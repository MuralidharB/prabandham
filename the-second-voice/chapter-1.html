Chapter One

Impulse Response

The first thing Mara Voss did every morning was check her phone.

Not for messages — or not only for messages. Primarily for the time, which told her how many minutes she had before the next thing, which told her how to feel about the morning, which was the closest she came these days to knowing how she felt about anything.

5:47. Thirteen minutes before the alarm. She turned it off, which she always did, and lay in the dark of the hotel room assembling the day in her head. The site visit was at nine. She had measurements to review before that — the impulse response data from yesterday's preliminary session, which she'd transferred to her laptop but not yet opened, because by the time she'd gotten back to the hotel she was running a conference call with the Chicago office and then she'd eaten something from the minibar and then she was asleep.

The room was in Valletta. The city was in Malta. She had been here for four days and had seen of it: the hotel, the inside of a hire car, a heritage site under renovation, the inside of the hire car again, a restaurant that turned out to be closed and then the minibar.

She showered. She dressed. She opened her laptop and looked at the data.

The Hypogeum of Hal Saflieni was approximately five thousand six hundred years old and sat beneath a residential neighborhood in the south of Valletta like a held breath. Three levels of chambers carved from coralline limestone, the deepest reaching twelve meters below street level. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Access strictly limited to eighty visitors per day to protect the delicate microclimate. Her client — a heritage interpretation consultancy — had been commissioned to develop a visitor center that would give people something of the site's atmospheric quality without exposing the original to increased footfall. They'd hired Mara to tell them what the something was, acoustically speaking.

This was the kind of work she was good at. Quantifying the unquantifiable. Translating the way a space felt into numbers that an architect could use.

She looked at the impulse response data from the main chambers — the characteristic reverberation signatures, the frequency decay profiles, the subtle standing wave patterns in the Hall of Oracles on the second level. Standard underground acoustics. Hard reflective surfaces. Minimal absorption. Nothing anomalous.

She looked at the data from the Oracle Room.

Also nothing anomalous. A pronounced resonance peak at 110 Hz. She'd read about this in the literature before coming — it had been documented, discussed, published. The chamber resonated at that frequency in response to a male baritone voice. She'd produced the tone herself with a signal generator rather than her voice, measured it cleanly, filed it in the appropriate folder.

What the data did not contain — could not contain, she understood this, it was a signal generator and a measurement microphone and a laptop, it was not that kind of instrument — was what had happened while she was collecting it.

She closed the laptop.

She had been in the Oracle Room at 2:47 in the afternoon, which she knew because she documented everything with timestamps. The room was small — an alcove, really, carved into the rock with a curved ceiling that caught sound and returned it changed. She was setting up her second measurement position, crouched near the threshold, when she became aware that the room had gone quiet in a way that was different from simply being quiet.

She had spent fifteen years working with sound. She understood silence the way a sculptor understands negative space — as a material, with properties, with texture. The silence in recording studios was different from the silence in concert halls empty of audiences, which was different from the silence of the natural world, which was different from the silence in rooms where someone had recently died, which she knew from her mother's house seven years ago and had not thought about since.

This was a different silence again.

It lasted approximately four seconds. She would be imprecise about this later because her sense of duration had done something strange — the four seconds had the interior volume of a much longer period, as if each second had been opened and she could see inside it. Later still she would give up trying to quantify it.

What she heard, inside the silence, was nothing she could describe. Not a sound. Not a voice. More like: the noise she had not known she was living inside — a continuous low-frequency presence she had accommodated so thoroughly she had ceased to register it — was briefly interrupted. And in the interruption, something she recognized as herself, or as a part of herself that had been there once and wasn't anymore, was briefly audible.

Then the guide appeared in the doorway asking if she was alright, and the moment closed, and she was a professional in a chamber with her equipment and she said yes, fine, just finishing up, and she meant it, she was fine, she was always fine.

She flew back to Chicago that Thursday. She sat in the airport lounge with her noise-canceling headphones on and worked through three client emails and a preliminary scope document for a hospital commission in Seattle and ate a turkey sandwich without tasting it. She upgraded to business class with points she had accumulated from living the majority of her life at thirty-seven thousand feet and slept without dreaming for six hours and landed at O'Hare into November rain and took a cab home.

Her apartment was clean in the particular way of spaces rarely inhabited — not the clean of someone who cleaned, but the clean of a place that had no opportunity to become dirty. She had a very good coffee machine. She made coffee, sat at her kitchen table, opened her laptop, and looked at the Malta data again. The 110 Hz resonance peak, well-documented, producing its effect in the Oracle Room as it had been producing its effect for fifty-six centuries. Nothing anomalous.

She went to bed.

She lay awake.

She lay awake the following night as well.

On the third night she identified what was wrong, which was this: she was waiting for something she couldn't name, and the waiting was the first feeling she'd had with any real depth of field in a period of time she didn't want to calculate. It felt less like hope than like the memory of hope — the somatic echo of a thing she had once had and, somewhere during fifteen years of airports and minibar dinners and meetings that could have been emails, had mislaid without noticing.

She was not unhappy. She wanted to be clear about this, at least to herself. She had a career that people admired and work that regularly required her best thinking and enough money and an apartment in a good neighborhood and friends she saw when scheduling allowed. She ran three times a week. She read on planes. She had, after Tom, after the relationship that had ended not badly but thoroughly, the way a door closes when neither person is angry enough to slam it — she had relearned how to sleep alone without the reaching. She was fine.

She booked a return flight to Malta on the fourth night.

There was a woman she needed to speak to.

This was not a rational conclusion. It was something she'd found in the Malta client file, which she had read thoroughly as she always read her client files: a reference, in the site's conservation notes, to a local practitioner named Elisabetta Pace who had worked with the Hypogeum's administration informally for decades and was, according to the notes, a source of certain kinds of institutional memory that the official record didn't contain. Not an academic. A nurse, retired. A Valletta native. There was a phone number.

Mara had no reason to call this number. Her commission did not require oral histories or community consultation. She was there to measure acoustics.

She called the number.

The phone rang three times. Then a woman's voice, low and unhurried, with the Maltese English that sounded like Italian deciding to become something else.

"You were in the Oracle Room," the woman said, before Mara had finished saying who she was or why she was calling. Not a question. "You heard something."

Mara was sitting at her kitchen table in Chicago with November rain on the windows and a cup of coffee going cold and fifteen years of professional composure at her disposal.

"Yes," she said. "I think I did."

A pause. The quality of the pause was deliberate, she felt — Elisabetta Pace pausing the way someone pauses who is deciding how honest to be, and deciding to be very.

"Good," said the woman. "Come back then. Come on Tuesday. I'll make lunch."

✦ ✦ ✦

She told her assistant she needed to extend the Malta engagement for additional measurement sessions. Her assistant, who had worked with her for four years and had long ago learned to ask the minimum necessary questions, booked the flight.

She told herself she was following a professional instinct — that the 110 Hz resonance warranted more detailed investigation, that the anomalous character of the Oracle Room's acoustic signature might have implications for the visitor center design that she hadn't fully mapped. She wrote this in an internal project note. It was not untrue. It was also not the reason.

The reason was that four seconds in a limestone chamber had given her back something she hadn't known she was missing, and it was gone again, and she didn't know how to get back to it by herself.

She ran on Sunday morning without her earbuds for the first time in approximately two years. She ran her usual route — the lakefront path, the dark water, the November light doing what November light in Chicago did, which was arrive obliquely and apologetically and still somehow be beautiful. Without the podcast she usually put on, without the playlist she'd had since 2019, she could hear her own breathing and the wind and the particular sound of the city at six in the morning, which was not silence but was the quietest thing she could access here, and she ran toward it with something she would not have called urgency but that moved in her body the same way.

On Tuesday she flew back to Malta.

She had, packed in her equipment case between the measurement microphone and the laptop and the coiled cables and the signal generator, a notebook she had bought on Sunday — paper, unlined, the kind she used to carry before everything became a screen. She had not yet written anything in it. She brought it because it seemed important to bring it, which was not the kind of reason she usually trusted, but she was forty-eight hours into trusting a different set of reasons, and this was where it had gotten her.

✦ ✦ ✦

Elisabetta Pace lived in a small apartment on a narrow street in Valletta that looked, from outside, like every other narrow street in Valletta, and felt, from inside, like a room that had been inhabited thoughtfully for a very long time. There were tomatoes growing on the balcony, which was unexpected in November and then, looking at the way they grew, not unexpected at all. There were books on every surface. There was a cat of advanced age and impressive indifference. There was coffee already made and bread from that morning and olives from somewhere.

The woman herself was seventy-one years old and built with the density of someone who had spent decades in proximity to the things that mattered — birth, death, and the infinite terrain between them. She had the hands of someone who had worked with her hands. She moved with precision rather than haste, which Mara recognized as a quality she had once had and had traded, without being fully aware of the transaction, for speed.

They ate. Elisabetta asked about the project — straightforward professional questions, asked with genuine interest, and Mara found herself answering with more honesty than she'd put into a conversation in some time, because the quality of the listening required it. This was the first of several things she would find difficult to quantify about Elisabetta Pace: the way she listened — not waiting for her own turn to speak, but actually receiving what was being said, which turned out to be unusual enough to be disorienting.

When Mara finished describing the project, Elisabetta poured more coffee and said: "Tell me about the four seconds."

Mara told her. Precisely, then less precisely, then in ways she hadn't intended to say because the precise version kept running out of instruments. Elisabetta listened throughout without interrupting, without the slight forward lean and softened expression that people produced when they were about to tell you what your experience meant, without anything except attention.

When Mara finished, Elisabetta was quiet for a moment. Then she said: "You design sounds for spaces. You understand that sound shapes how people feel inside a room."

"Yes."

"And you spend your life doing this for other people."

"That's the work, yes."

"When did you last design a space for yourself?"

Mara opened her mouth. Closed it.

"Not a room," Elisabetta said. "I mean — when did you last make the conditions for yourself to hear something that wasn't managed? That wasn't chosen from a menu?"

Outside the narrow window the city made its sounds — scooters, voices, stone carrying everything the way stone did. Mara was aware, sitting in this room with this woman she had met an hour ago, that she didn't have an answer to the question. Not a good one. The answer was approximately: not since she could remember. The answer was: she had spent fifteen years becoming exceptionally skilled at the acoustic management of other people's experiences and had somewhere in the same period stopped having any of her own.

"The room found you," Elisabetta said. "The Oracle Room. It has been doing this for a very long time — finding people whose silence has become loud enough to hear. You came as a professional. You stayed as something else."

"I'm not sure what I stayed as."

"No. You won't be sure for a while yet." Elisabetta said this with the matter-of-fact warmth of someone reporting a weather pattern — something coming, prepare appropriately. "There are others. Other places. The room in Malta was the beginning, not the whole."

She wrote a name on a piece of paper. Tarık Aşkın. A city: Şanlıurfa, southeastern Turkey.

"He doesn't know you're coming," Elisabetta said. "But he'll know you when you arrive. He's been waiting, as I've been waiting. As all of us have been waiting."

Mara looked at the name on the paper. "Waiting for what?"

Elisabetta picked up her coffee cup. Outside, a church bell measured the afternoon in the way church bells in ancient cities measured everything — unhurried, indifferent to whether you were listening, sounding anyway.

"For someone who heard something," she said. "And came back."

✦ ✦ ✦

That night, in the hotel room, Mara opened the unlined notebook and wrote the date at the top of the first page.

Below it she wrote: Four seconds.

Below that she wrote, after a long time: I have been very loud for a very long time.

She looked at this. It was not precise. It was not quantifiable. It was the truest thing she had written in years.

She turned off the light. Through the window of the hotel room, the Mediterranean was invisible in the darkness but present — she could hear it, the water moving against the ancient stone of the harbor walls with the patience of something that had been doing this since before there were walls, since before there was anyone to build them or anyone to notice the sound.

She lay in the dark and, for the first time in as long as she could accurately remember, did not put anything on to fill the silence.

She listened.

The sea moved.

She slept.
