Chapter Six

The Floor

The bus from Huaraz left at five in the morning and climbed for three hours through darkness into the Cordillera Blanca, the road narrowing as the altitude increased until it was a single lane of unpaved switchbacks pressed between the mountain on one side and nothing on the other — a darkness that, when she looked into it, resolved slowly into the shapes of valley and river and the far white peaks catching the first light at an elevation where the air was too thin to hold color properly and everything was silver and absolute. She was the only foreigner on the bus. She had a window seat and a bag on her lap and the piece of granite in her jacket pocket and she held it for most of the three hours, not as comfort exactly but as orientation — the worked face warm against her thumb, the evidence of attention, someone once having listened to a material and the material having responded.

Rodrigo Quispe had not told her how to find him. He had replied to her email — passed through Chen Liwei, who passed it through the same informal chain that now connected all of them, the network Mara was assembling without having planned to assemble it — with an address that turned out to be a village of forty houses at 3,200 meters, and a time: eight in the morning, at the square. There was one square. There was one man sitting at its edge when the bus arrived, drinking something from a tin cup, watching the road with the patience of someone for whom waiting was not a condition imposed from outside but a practice cultivated from within.

He was sixty-three years old and looked it without apology — a face that had been at altitude for six decades, weathered and particular, with eyes that held the specific quality she had come to recognize across four continents and five Carriers: presence. Not charisma, not performance, not the organized warmth of someone who had learned to put people at ease. Simply the quality of someone who was entirely where they were, which turned out, in a world of people who were mostly somewhere else, to be extraordinary.

He looked at her for a moment. Then he poured her a cup from the thermos — coca tea, bitter and restorative — and said, in Spanish she was managing with the help of the phrasebook she had studied on the flight from Chengdu: "You've had a long journey."

"From Chicago," she said. "By way of Malta, Turkey, Egypt, Zimbabwe, China."

He nodded as if this were a reasonable itinerary. "And now you are here."

"And now I'm here."

He looked at her a moment longer, and she had the sense of being assessed by someone whose assessments had very little to do with the usual criteria — not credentials, not fluency, not the performance of readiness. Something less definable and more reliable. Then he stood, collected the thermos, and said: "Come tomorrow morning. We will talk first. Then we will see."

She spent the rest of that first day in the village, which was not difficult because the village did not offer many alternatives. She walked the perimeter of it, which took twenty minutes. She sat beside the river that ran below it — glacial melt, gray-green and cold enough to ache — and watched the water and thought about Chen Liwei's glaciers losing themselves decade by decade into a watershed that could no longer be sustained by what remained, and felt the proximity of that loss in the specific temperature of the water against her fingers, which she had put in without deciding to, the way you test the reality of a thing you are having trouble fully believing.

She ate dinner at the one establishment that served food — a woman named Doña Carmen who fed her potato soup and bread and asked, through gestures more than language, how long she was staying. She held up three fingers. Doña Carmen held up five and raised an eyebrow. Mara considered this. She held up five.

She slept at altitude with the particular quality of sleep that thin air produces: deep and dreamless and complete, as if the body, deprived of excess oxygen, had shed everything unnecessary and arrived at something essential. She woke before dawn to silence so absolute it had a texture. She lay in it for a long time without reaching for anything to fill it.

Rodrigo talked to her for three hours the next morning, seated on low wooden chairs in the room at the front of his house that served as a consulting space — a curandero's room, practical and unsentimental, with dried plants in bundles along one wall and a small table and two chairs and a window that looked at the mountain.

He talked about his work the way Tarık had talked about hydrology — as a matter of fact, as something that required precision and honesty above all things, as a practice that was also a responsibility. He treated people. People with grief, with addiction, with depression, with what he called, in the Spanish she was slowly assembling into comprehension, la enfermedad moderna — the modern sickness, the specific pathology of disconnection from one's own life, which he saw in his community and saw increasingly in the people who came from outside it. He used plants. He used sound — specific instruments played in specific sequences in enclosed spaces. He used darkness and proximity to the stone galleries in the natural formations near the river. He was not mystical about any of this. He described it the way a surgeon describes a procedure: accurately, without embellishment, with a clear account of what it was intended to achieve.

"What it achieves," he said, "is the temporary interruption of the story."

"Which story?"

"The story the self tells about itself. Continuously. Every human being is running a continuous internal narration — who they are, what they deserve, what threatens them, what they want, what they have failed at, what they must protect. This narration is not the self. But it presents itself as the self, and after enough years it becomes very difficult to distinguish between the narrator and the person it is narrating." He looked at her steadily. "You understand this. I can see that you do."

She understood it the way she had come to understand several things on this journey — first as information, then as recognition, then as the particular discomfort of recognition becoming specific and personal rather than abstractly true.

"The narration stops," she said. "In the galleries."

"The narration is interrupted. It returns. But in the interruption — which lasts longer than the Oracle Room, longer than the hilltop, because the conditions here are more sustained — there is something available that was not available before. The self without the story. Which is frightening, because the story has been all we knew of ourselves for so long that its absence feels like annihilation." He paused. "It is not annihilation. It is the floor. What is there when everything constructed has been temporarily removed."

"And what is there?"

"That is what you will find out," he said. "I cannot tell you. It is different for each person, and the telling would pollute the finding. What I can tell you is that it is not nothing. That is what people fear most going in — that if the story stops there will be nothing underneath it. There is something. There has always been something. The story was simply too loud to hear it."

She was quiet for a moment. Through the window the mountain was doing what mountains at altitude did in the morning: receiving the light slowly, reluctantly, as if light were an intrusion on a privacy it had been maintaining since before there was anyone to observe it.

"I'm frightened," she said. She said it simply, as information, the way she had learned from all of them to say true things without performing them.

"Good," Rodrigo said. "People who are not frightened have usually not understood what they have agreed to. The frightened ones tend to do better."

She said no on the first day. She came back the next morning and said no again. He received both refusals without comment — without the slight recalibration she'd half-expected, the adjustment of posture or expression that would signal disappointment. Her refusal was her business. He had other things to attend to. He was not waiting for her to be ready; he was simply available when she was.

She spent those two days walking the terrain around the village. She found, on the afternoon of the second day, the river canyon where the natural stone galleries Rodrigo had described were formed — a narrowing of the gorge where the water had, over millennia, carved chambers into the cliff face, smooth-walled and resonant, the sound of the river entering them and being transformed by the stone into something more complex and less locatable, coming from everywhere and nowhere simultaneously. She stood at the entrance of one for a long time without going in. She could hear what the stone was doing to the sound. It was the most sophisticated natural acoustic environment she had ever encountered — the cliff geometry producing a standing wave pattern that her trained ear recognized as deliberate, though it was the river and the rock that had made it, across thousands of years, without intention.

Or: without the kind of intention she usually meant by the word.

She went back to Rodrigo's consulting room on the morning of the third day and sat down and said: "Today."

He looked at her for a moment. "Yes," he said. "Today."

What she could write in her notebook afterward, and did, over three attempts across two days, was this:

The galleries were dark in a way that had no gradations — not the adjustable dark of a room with curtains, but the complete dark of enclosed stone at depth, a dark that did not resolve into shapes as the eye adjusted because there was nothing for the eye to find. She had expected to manage this. She was not afraid of the dark in ordinary terms — she had worked in unlit recording spaces, in acoustic chambers, in underground structures. But this dark was different because it was ancient and because she was in it alone and because the sound Rodrigo made at the entrance, before she went in, with an instrument she couldn't identify — a sustained low tone that the galleries received and elaborated into harmonics she could feel in her sternum — had already begun doing something to the story.

She sat. The stone was cold through her clothing. She held the granite piece in both hands — not consciously; she found it there, already in her palms, sometime after she had stopped tracking the sequence of events that had brought her to sitting.

The sound came through the walls. Not from outside — from the stone itself, which was conducting and transforming the acoustic energy Rodrigo was producing at the entrance and returning it through the gallery chambers as something that did not have a source she could locate. It was at frequencies below what she would normally call sound and above what she would call silence. She felt it in her jaw and her solar plexus and the long bones of her legs. Her analytical mind, which had been her primary instrument for thirty-eight years, identified the acoustic mechanism — infrasound, standing waves, the resonant amplification of the stone cavity — and then, lacking anything further it could usefully do with the information, went quiet.

She did not know what happened next in terms that would satisfy her professional self. She knew only — and this knowing was bodily, cellular, the kind of knowing that precedes language by a very long way — that the narration stopped. Not gradually. Not the way attention drifts and refocuses. More like a light going out: on, then not.

What was there in the absence was not peace. It was not the tranquil emptiness of meditation as she had vaguely imagined it. It was a quality of attention so clean and so merciless that it functioned like grief — it showed her, without flinching and without the mitigating apparatus of the narration, everything she had been looking away from.

Her sister. The phone calls she hadn't made, across two years, across ten years if she was honest, the slow withdrawal from someone who loved her driven not by conflict but by the effort of maintaining contact with someone who saw her more clearly than she was comfortable being seen. She had called once from Zimbabwe and wept and not explained. She had not called since.

Tom. The relationship that had ended not badly but thoroughly, the door closing quietly, and what she had not admitted then and could not avoid now: that he had been right. She had not been present. She had been there in body and largely elsewhere in every other sense, and she had found his observation unfair because acknowledging it would have required her to ask where she actually was, which was a question she hadn't been ready to survive.

The work. Fifteen years of it, genuinely good work, work that required her intelligence and her specialized perception and her capacity to translate between the measurable and the felt — and at the center of it, the thing she had not looked at: she had stopped loving it. Not recently. The love had gone out slowly, replaced by proficiency, and proficiency had been sufficient and she had not investigated the difference.

She saw all of this not with shame and not with the softening of retrospective sympathy but simply as it was — as the terrain of a life from which she had been largely absent, tended by the industrious and efficient and entirely hollow version of herself that had kept everything running while she was somewhere else that she could not now identify. She had been somewhere else. The self without the story did not know where. The story had been the alibi; without it, the absence was simply absence, undisguised and complete.

She cried for a long time in the dark. Not performance — there was no one to perform for. The stone received it with the same indifference it received everything: acoustically, faithfully, without judgment.

At some point the sound from the entrance changed — a shift in frequency that she registered physically before consciously — and she understood it was time. She found her way out by the faint gradient of light at the gallery's mouth, which appeared gradually as she moved toward it, the dark becoming less absolute by degrees until she was in the gray of the gorge in the afternoon, sitting on a flat stone by the river, the water running past her with its glacial cold and its indifference and its tremendous ongoing commitment to reaching wherever it was going.

Rodrigo was sitting nearby. Not close. Present.

He said nothing for a long time. Then: "That is the glacier retreating. Your own glacier. It has been retreating for years. Now you can see what it was covering."

She looked at the river. Gray-green. Cold. Running from something that was diminishing toward something that needed it.

"What do I do with it?" she asked. Not rhetorically. Actually asking.

"Nothing yet," he said. "Sit with it first. The narration will come back — it always does, it is not destroyed, only interrupted — and when it does it will try to organize what you found into a story it can manage. Your work is to not let it do that too quickly. To hold what you saw without immediately translating it into a plan." He looked at the water. "The plan will come. But the plan that comes after you have seen clearly is a different plan than the one you would have made before."

She stayed for five days, as Doña Carmen had predicted.

She went into the galleries once more, on the fourth day, for a shorter duration and without the sustained acoustic preparation — alone, with only the river's natural elaboration in the stone. What she found on the second visit was quieter and, in its way, more sustaining: not the violent clarity of the first time but a gentler version of the same quality, the floor accessible now without the story's complete removal, something she could stand on while the story was still present. A beginning of integration.

She and Rodrigo talked for hours across the five days — in Spanish that improved daily, in silence that was always productive, in the particular register that had characterized every conversation on this journey: the one in which true things were said plainly without the social machinery that usually surrounded them. He told her about his son in Lima, who was kind and successful and embarrassed by his father in professional contexts. He told her about the people he treated, and what he saw in them, and what the cure was when there was one. He told her that the cure was almost always the same: not the ceremony in the galleries, which was the beginning, but the decision to keep attending to what the ceremony had revealed, which was the work, which continued for the rest of a life if you did it seriously.

"The monuments," she said on the last evening, sitting outside in the cold Andean dark, the stars at this altitude doing what the stars over Zimbabwe had done — asserting their actual nature rather than the pale suggestion of it that light pollution permitted. "They were built for this. All of them. To produce this interruption."

"Yes," he said. "In different ways, by different means, for the specific people and the specific landscape in which they were built. The acoustic properties of the Hypogeum are not the same as the visual instruction of the Sanxingdui masks, which are not the same as what happens in the natural galleries here. But the target is the same. The same interruption. The same floor."

"And the world we have built," she said. "Is a world without the interruption."

"A world that has made the interruption structurally unavailable," he said. "Not through malice. Through the preference, across many small decisions, for the narration over the floor. The narration is more comfortable. The narration gives us plans and identities and the assurance that we are managing our lives. The floor is only the truth." He looked at the stars. "And we have built, over centuries, a civilization whose entire infrastructure is designed to keep us comfortable and keep us from the truth. Every screen, every notification, every filled silence — all of it in the service of the narration. Keeping it running. Preventing the interruption."

"Because the interruption shows us what we're doing," she said.

"Because the interruption shows us what we're doing," he confirmed. "And what we are doing, looked at clearly, is—" He stopped. Not because he didn't have the word. Because the word was sufficient and did not need amplification. "Difficult to continue with, if you have really seen it."

She held the granite in her hand. The Andean night pressed close, enormous, full of the sounds of a landscape that had been doing what it was doing for geological time and would continue regardless of what happened at the altitude where human beings had established themselves and begun, with enormous ingenuity and thoroughness, to make the world unlivable.

Before she left he gave her a name: Valentina Cruz, Mexico City. An urban planner. She should contact her through the municipal planning office — she worked there openly, without concealment, because her lineage had decided long ago that the knowledge was most safely kept in plain sight.

He also said something she had not expected and which she would carry to every remaining site and beyond: "You are not the first to make this journey. There have been others, over the centuries — people who found one or two of the keepers and received fragments and then stopped, or were stopped. You are the first, as far as I know, to have assembled this many fragments in a single passage." He looked at her steadily. "I do not know what that means. None of us do. We only know that we have been waiting for it and that you are it and that what you do with what you carry is not something any of us can tell you."

She took the bus back down through the switchbacks in the early morning, the valley revealing itself in the opposite direction — from cloud and altitude and the white peaks releasing their light into a landscape that widened and warmed as she descended, the gray glacial green of the river accompanying her, reduced, committed, running toward its appointment with the lowlands and the sea.

She did not open her notebook on the bus. She did not put her headphones on. She sat with the window cold against her forehead and the piece of granite in her hand and the enormity of what she had found in the dark sitting in her chest, not yet a plan, not yet a language, not yet anything the narration could manage — still the raw material of something, uncut, uncategorized, present the way the granite had been present in the cliff before Fatima's lineage learned to hear what it wanted to become.

She held it. She did not reach for anything to put between herself and it.

This was, she was beginning to understand, the entire practice. Not the ceremony, not the ancient sites, not even the Carriers and what they had given her. Those were the preparation. The practice was this: the willingness to hold what you had found without immediately converting it into something more manageable. The willingness to let it be what it was, for as long as it needed, before you decided what to do with it.

The floor, she thought. This is the floor.

She was still there when the bus reached Huaraz and the altitude fell to something the body recognized as ordinary and the air thickened and the narration returned, gently, with the consideration of something that knew it had lost some ground and was not certain how much.

Less than before, she told it silently. That is what has changed. Not that you are gone. That I know the difference now between you and the floor. And that is a difference I will not unknow.

She booked a flight to Mexico City. She called her sister first.

Her sister answered on the second ring, which meant she had been waiting, which meant she had always been waiting, which was one of the things the floor had shown her that she was not yet ready to look at for the full duration but was, for the first time, willing to look at at all.

"Hi," she said. Her voice was different. She heard it herself — lower, slower, coming from somewhere less defended.

Her sister heard it too. There was a pause, and in the pause the specific quality of someone recalibrating toward something they had hoped for and stopped expecting.

"Hi," her sister said. And then: "Where are you?"

"Peru," Mara said. "I'll explain when I'm back. I'm not back for a while yet. But I wanted to call."

"I'm glad you called," her sister said. Simply. Without the weight of the years it had taken to get here. The grace of that — of someone who loved her meeting her exactly where she arrived, without making her account for the distance — was something she would not be able to write in the notebook without crying, so she didn't try. She held the phone and the granite simultaneously and talked to her sister for an hour while the Andes arranged themselves behind her into something that looked, in the late afternoon light, almost like a farewell.
