Chapter Nine

Are You Ready to Be Watched?

The flight from Santiago took five hours and landed at the end of the world.

This was not metaphor. Easter Island — Rapa Nui — sat alone in the South Pacific at a distance from the nearest inhabited land so extreme that the mind declined to fully process it, filing it instead under the category of facts that were technically understood and experientially unavailable. She had read the number: 3,500 kilometers to the Chilean coast, 4,000 to Tahiti, 2,600 to Pitcairn, which was itself one of the most isolated places on earth. From the plane's window, descending through cloud, she saw first the ocean — the Pacific in every direction, green-gray and enormous and without feature — and then the island itself, sudden and small and impossibly green for something so alone, a triangle of volcanic land that forty generations of a single people had called home and learned to the depth that only absolute isolation and absolute commitment can produce.

Mana Paoa was waiting at the small airport with the unhurried certainty of someone who had nowhere else to be, which was literally and also philosophically true — she had been born on this island and had never left it and had never wanted to, and this was not limitation but knowledge, the knowledge of someone who understood that forty generations of presence in a single place produced an intimacy with that place that no amount of travel could substitute for. She was fifty-five years old, built with the solidity of her Polynesian lineage, and she regarded Mara with eyes that had the particular quality Mara had learned, across eight sites and eight Carriers, to recognize immediately: the quality of someone who was not performing their attention but actually giving it.

She shook Mara's hand without speaking. Then she took her bag — a gesture so matter-of-fact that declining it would have been absurd — put it in the back of an old pickup truck, and drove her not to the guesthouse but directly to the site, which Mara understood was not impatience but priority. The island knew she was here. It had been waiting longer than Mana had.

The quarry at Rano Raraku occupied the inner and outer slopes of an extinct volcanic crater in the island's southeast, and what it contained was one of the most quietly devastating landscapes Mara had ever stood in. Hundreds of moai in every stage of incompletion — some barely begun, their forms barely suggested in the volcanic tuff of the crater wall; some nearly finished, their massive faces emerging from the rock with the specificity of portraiture, each one particular, each one a person rather than a type; some fully carved and standing in the slope's soft soil where they had been set to wait while their eyes were prepared and their transportation arranged, and where they had simply remained when the civilization that made them ceased to be able to move them.

They stood in the slope at angles, settled into the earth over six centuries, some buried to the chin, some to the shoulder, their great stone faces looking across the Pacific with the patience of things that had stopped waiting because waiting was no longer the condition — that had simply become what they were, standing figures in a hillside, present without purpose, carrying their makers' intention intact through six hundred years of wind and rain and the indifferent continuation of the world.

Mara stood among them in the late afternoon and felt something she had not felt at any preceding site — not the disruption of the Oracle Room, not the structured gravity of Göbekli Tepe, not the political weight of Great Zimbabwe, not the instruction of the bronze masks, not the violence of the Andean galleries, not the pattern-completing clarity of the Avenue of the Dead, not the carver's demand of the Kailash sanctuary. What she felt here was simpler and in some ways harder than all of those. She felt recognized.

Not by the moai — she was careful about this, careful not to import mysticism into something that wanted only honesty. Not recognized by the statues. Recognized by the situation. Standing among hundreds of unfinished things on a hillside where a civilization had worked with complete commitment right up to the moment it could no longer work, she felt the specific recognition of someone who had spent four months learning to see clearly finally standing before the thing that was asking to be seen most clearly of all.

She knew this island's story. She had known it before she came, and knowing it had not prepared her for what standing inside it felt like.

The Rapa Nui had watched their forest disappear. The pollen record was unambiguous — the island had been densely forested when they arrived, a thousand years ago, and the trees had gone steadily as the population grew and the moai construction accelerated, each statue requiring timber for the rollers and ropes that transported it, each transported statue requiring more trees, the forest retreating as the carving intensified, two processes locked in a relationship whose outcome was arithmetically inevitable and which the people living inside it had somehow been unable to see, or unable to act on seeing, which came to the same end.

This was what she had come here to understand. Not as history. As a mirror.

Mana had not yet spoken. She stood nearby with the quality Mara associated with the best of the Carriers — presence without intrusion, available without pressure — and waited while the quarry did what it had been doing for six centuries, which was simply being what it was, unmediated, without explanation or apology, the evidence of a civilization's limit offered to whoever was willing to stand before it and receive it honestly.

After a long time, Mara said: "They could see it happening."

"Yes," Mana said.

"And they couldn't stop."

"They could not stop alone. No individual stopped it, because stopping required a collective decision to put down the ritual and take up survival, and no community finds it easy to make that decision once the ritual has become the primary reality. The ritual felt more real than the trees. The moai were more present to them than the forest's absence." She paused. "I do not say this as condemnation. I say it as description. We are describing the present moment."

Mara looked at the faces in the hillside. Each one particular. Each one carrying someone's specific vision of an ancestor — the slightly different set of the jaw, the particular weight of the brow — rendered permanent in volcanic tuff and then stopped, mid-becoming, by the collapse of the system that had given them purpose. They had not been abandoned. Abandonment implies a decision. These had been simply overtaken by the end of the capacity to continue.

"The narration," she said. Rodrigo's word, the one that had been most precise. "The ritual was a narration. It told them who they were — a people who honored their ancestors through the building of monuments. And when the narration became loud enough, the forest stopped being the condition of their survival and became simply the material cost of continuing the story."

"Yes," Mana said. "And the story was not false. The ancestors were real. The honoring was real. The moai are extraordinary — I have stood among them my whole life and they have not become ordinary to me. What was false was not the story but the inability to hear anything else while the story was running." She looked at the hillside. "The deafness was not to the ancestors. The deafness was to the trees."

Mana took her, the following morning, to the coastal platform at Ahu Tongariki — the largest ceremonial site on the island, fifteen moai restored to their platform after being toppled by a tsunami in 1960, standing again since the 1990s, facing inland as all the moai had faced, their backs to the sea, their eyes directed at the living community they had been built to watch over.

The eyes, Mana explained, standing before the restored figures in the early light, were last. The moai were blind until coral irises were placed in the carved sockets — white coral for the whites, red scoria for the pupils — at which point they were considered awakened. Inhabited. Present. Not merely stone but the ancestors themselves, returned to their watching-over of the community that had built them.

"The installation of the eyes was not a ceremony of completion," Mana said. "It was a ceremony of readiness. The community had to decide, together, that it was prepared to be watched. That it had been living in a manner its ancestors could acknowledge. A moai without eyes was not a failure of construction — it was a statement that the community was not yet ready for that accountability."

The fifteen restored figures stood against the morning sky with their coral eyes in place, looking inland, looking past Mara and Mana and the handful of other visitors and the roads and the tourism infrastructure and the 97% deforested hillsides to whatever the concept of community meant now on this island that had been so thoroughly changed by what it had done to itself and what had subsequently been done to it.

"Are they watching now?" Mara asked. She asked it seriously, as a genuine question, and Mana received it as such.

"They are doing what they were built to do," Mana said. "Whether anyone is prepared to be watched is a different question."

She let this sit. The light was very clear at this hour — the Pacific clarity that came from being so far from any other land mass that the air arrived unprocessed, carrying nothing from elsewhere, showing everything in its actual condition.

"My family has been on this island for forty generations," Mana said. "Forty generations of people who watched the quarry and the forests and the ceremony and the collapse and then the silence and then the arrival of the outsiders and what they brought and what they took and the slow, partial, genuinely difficult work of recovering something from all of that." She was quiet for a moment. "What the family held through all of this was not the technique of the moai — that knowledge was lost and has been partially reconstructed through archaeology. What was held was the understanding of what the eyes meant. Why they went in last. What kind of community had to exist before the eyes could be placed."

"A community willing to be accountable," Mara said.

"A community willing to be accountable to everything that came before and everything that would come after. Not to an institution, not to a law, not to a set of rules administered by someone else. To the actual presence of the ancestors, understood as the concentrated wisdom of everyone who had lived on this land before you, and to the actual existence of the descendants, understood as the people who would have to live with what you did." Mana looked at the restored figures. "This is what we have lost. Not the statues — the statues can be restored, as you see. What cannot be restored by archaeology is the quality of attention that made the community decide, together, that it was ready to be watched. That it had been living worthily of watching."

Mara stood before the fifteen figures and felt the question arrive — not as Mana's question, not as the island's question, but as her own, the one she had been approaching across nine sites and four months, the one that all the preceding questions had been preparing her to receive.

Are you ready to be watched?

Not by ancestors in the literal sense. Not by stone figures on a Pacific platform. But by the quality of attention the question pointed toward — the willingness to live as though everything you did was visible to the people who came before you and the people who would come after, to hold that visibility as a constant rather than an occasional consideration, to make each decision in the full presence of that accountability rather than inside the narration that managed it away.

She held the granite piece. She had been carrying it for eight sites. It was warm, worked smooth, the evidence of someone who had listened to a material and the material had responded.

"I don't know," she said honestly. "I don't know if I'm ready. I don't know if anyone is ever fully ready."

"No," Mana said. "The eyes go in before the community is certain. The installation is the commitment, not the confirmation. You decide to be ready and then you live toward the readiness." She looked at Mara. "You have been deciding, since Malta. Each site has been the placement of something. Not the eyes — the preparation for the eyes. This is the last site. This is where you decide."

She spent three days on the island. She walked every morning before sunrise — the roads that crossed the deforested plateau, the coastal paths where the cliff faces dropped into the Pacific, the interior tracks that wound among the grassy hills that had once been forest and now carried only the ghost of what they'd been in the unusual richness of their soil, the fertility accumulated across centuries of decomposition that the trees were no longer present to use.

She walked in the way she had learned to walk — attending. Not managing. Not filling. Letting the island arrive at whatever pace it chose, in whatever form it had available, which was a form deeply and irreversibly shaped by what had happened here but not exhausted by it. The island was 97% deforested and still beautiful. The soil was eroding and the restoration projects were partial and the causes were complex and the outcomes uncertain and the island was still alive with the specific vitality of a place that had not given up, that continued doing what land did — holding what it could hold, growing what it could grow, offering to the sea and the sky and the occasional visitor whatever it had remaining, which was not nothing.

On the second evening Mana took her to the quarry at sunset. They sat among the unfinished moai while the light changed over the Pacific — orange to rose to the specific deep blue that preceded the stars, which arrived at this latitude and this distance from any other light source with a density that had become, across four months of ancient sites, something she simply expected from darkness. The sky as it actually was, rather than the diminished version of it that the illuminated world permitted.

Mana said: "My grandfather's grandfather's grandfather. Six generations back. He was the one who carried the memory of the eyes through the period when the island had nothing — when the population had collapsed and the missionaries had arrived and the traditions were being actively dismantled and the language was being lost and there was no reason, by any external measure, to keep holding what he held." She looked at the faces in the hillside, their sightless stone eyes aimed at the darkening sky. "He held it because he understood that the memory of what a community could be was worth preserving even when no such community currently existed. That the knowledge of the eyes mattered even when there was no one ready to place them."

"He held it for a future he couldn't see," Mara said. The phrase had the resonance of something she had been hearing in different forms since Göbekli Tepe — Tarık's buried hill, the nameless builders of Teotihuacan, Valentina's water plans, Anand's fathers' vision transmitted through hands laid over drawing hands. All of them holding forward something for a time they would not inhabit.

"He held it for you," Mana said simply. "He could not have known that. But here you are."

Mara was quiet for a long time. Around them the moai settled into the dark, their forms becoming less distinct as the light went, resolving into the shapes of the hillside, into the island itself, back into the volcanic material from which they had emerged six centuries ago with such astonishing particularity. She thought about the Rapa Nui who had made them — not as cautionary tale, not as warning, but as people. People with the specific human combination of extraordinary capacity and ordinary limitation. People who could see the trees disappearing and carve another face anyway, because the carving was how they understood themselves and understanding yourself is not something you lay down easily even when the cost is becoming visible.

She thought: I understand this now in a way I did not understand it in Malta.

She thought: this is what the journey was for.

Not to learn what the ancient world knew that the modern world had forgotten — though it was that. Not to be initiated into a lineage of hidden knowledge — though it was that too, in its way. The journey was for this: to arrive at a place where the mirror was so clear and so complete that looking away was simply no longer available. Where the island itself was the answer to the question she had been assembling since the Oracle Room — the answer not as information but as confrontation, as the specific face of what happened when a civilization stopped listening, rendered in stone and deforested hillside and the patient figures of the quarry standing in their slope for six centuries, waiting for someone to stop making enough noise to see what they were saying.

She saw it.

And seeing it, she understood what she would do.

On the last morning she made two calls.

The first was to the developer in Malta. The commission she had been avoiding since Peru — the commission to design the acoustic environment for the heritage resort that would permanently alter the landscape around the Hypogeum, that would capture the site's atmosphere as a product, that would be celebrated and award-winning and would signal to every developer in the Mediterranean that the acoustic heritage of the ancient world was available for appropriation by whoever arrived with the right budget. She had known she would decline it since Lima. She had not made the call because she had been waiting — for certainty, for the complete form of the second answer, for the readiness that Mana had described not as the confirmation of readiness but as the commitment to it.

She was ready to commit.

She made the call. The developer was professional and cool and did not ask for reasons, which meant he had already arranged the next candidate and was simply completing the courtesy of the closure. She did not volunteer reasons. She said she was declining and that she wished the project well and that she was grateful for the consideration. She said it without the internal apology she would have offered four months ago — the performance of regret designed to make the other person comfortable with her choice. She said it as what it was: a decision, made, irrevocable, the first cut.

The second call was to a number she had been carrying in her notebook since Chengdu — an acoustic conservation organization she had known about for years and engaged with never. She had met the director once at a conference in Amsterdam and found her compelling and then not followed up, in the way of her previous life, in which compelling things were noted and then managed into the future where they would be engaged with when there was time and there was never time.

The director answered. Mara said who she was. Then she said what she was proposing: a comprehensive acoustic impact assessment for UNESCO World Heritage Sites, to be used as the scientific foundation for a legal protection framework. Pro bono. Beginning immediately. She had the professional expertise to do it and the network of site-specific knowledge she had spent four months accumulating and the specific understanding of what acoustic degradation meant for sites built around acoustic precision, which was different from and more urgent than what the current heritage protection frameworks addressed.

There was a pause.

Then the director said: "We have been trying to fund exactly this project for three years. We do not have the budget for someone of your caliber."

"I know," Mara said. "I said pro bono."

Another pause, shorter. "When can we meet?"

She named a date. She put the phone in her pocket and stood on the coastal path above the Pacific with the wind coming off the water at the temperature of the deep ocean, cold and clean and indifferent to what had just happened, which was small and not heroic and would not be noted anywhere and would not save anything by itself and was the only right response she had to the mirror the island had held up, and was enough. Was exactly enough.

One thing stopped. One thing started.

She said goodbye to Mana at the airport with the same simplicity with which she had been greeted — a handshake that was also something more, a recognition of what had passed between them that did not require ceremony because it was already contained in everything that had been said and not said across three days on the most isolated island on earth.

"What will you do," Mara asked, "when I am gone?"

Mana considered this in the literal sense, as she considered everything. "I will go back to work. There is a restoration project in the quarry — we are working with a team of archaeologists to properly document the unfinished moai before the soil erosion takes any more of the context. And there is a school program I run, for the children of the island, that takes them to each of the major sites and asks them to sit quietly in front of the moai for twenty minutes and then write about what they heard."

"What do they hear?"

"Different things. They are children — their narrations are not yet fully formed, which means the quieting is easier for them than it is for you." A slight warmth in this, the warmth that had been building across three days and had arrived at last, unhurried, at its own pace. "Some of them hear the wind. Some of them hear the island. Some of them hear their own breathing, for the first time, which surprises them." She paused. "One girl, last year, said she heard her great-great-grandmother. I did not tell her this was impossible. I asked her what her great-great-grandmother said."

Mara waited.

"She said: the girl told her to stop wasting the quiet."

She flew back to Santiago and then to Miami and then to Chicago, which she reached on a gray January morning with the lake frozen at its edges and the city doing what Chicago did in January, which was continue regardless, which was admirable and exhausting in equal measure and which she found, returning to it after four months away, both familiar and genuinely strange — the city she had lived in for a decade, seen now as if she had been away long enough for the seeing to be new.

She retrieved her apartment from the subletter, who had left it clean and slightly rearranged in ways she found she didn't mind. She bought groceries — real ones, attended to in the market with the quality of attention she had spent four months learning and was not yet fluent in and was committed to practicing. She unpacked her bag and put the equipment case in the corner and set the notebook on the kitchen table and placed the piece of pink granite beside it, the worked face up, warm in the winter light from the window.

She did not put a podcast on when she ran the following morning. She ran the lakefront path in the January cold with the frozen lake on her left and the city on her right and the sky overhead doing whatever the sky did — she was no longer managing her relationship with weather any more than with anything else — and she listened to the particular acoustic character of Chicago in winter, which was different from the city's summer voice, quieter in some registers and sharper in others, the cold doing something to the way sound traveled through the air that her professional training could account for and her recovering personal attention simply received.

She ran without filling the silence.

It was not easy. The habit was very old and the alternative was very new and the run lasted forty minutes in which she was sometimes present and sometimes not and sometimes aware of the difference and sometimes not, which was not failure but the actual texture of a practice that would last the rest of her life and would not resolve into mastery, only into a better and better relationship with the attempt.

That evening she opened her laptop and composed nine emails.

Simple emails. The same email, essentially, adapted slightly for each recipient — for the different first languages, the different contexts, the different qualities of the relationships she had built across four months of this journey. She told each of them that there were eight others. She gave each of them the other eight names and locations. She did not explain what this meant or what they should do with it — she was not the architect of what happened next, had no authority to direct it, was simply the person who had moved between them and held all the fragments simultaneously and come out the other side with the knowledge that they existed and that they needed to know of each other.

She told them about the acoustic protection project. She asked if any of them would be willing to be consulted — not as participants in a scheme, not as members of an organization, simply as people with specific knowledge of specific sites that she would need to understand more deeply than a professional assessment alone could reach. She said they could say no. She said she would be grateful either way for what they had already given her, which was more than she had words adequate to.

She read each email twice. Then she sent them.

Then she sat at her kitchen table in the January dark and listened to the city outside her window — the sound of twenty-seven floors of a building full of people living their lives, which she could hear in the particular way of someone who had spent four months in proximity to what twelve thousand years of human attention sounded like, and which was not beautiful in the way the ancient sites were beautiful but was fully and irreducibly alive, still capable of everything the monuments had been built toward, still in possession of the floor even when the narration was loudest, still speaking in the register that managed attention could not receive and was available to attention that had been, by long practice and deliberate effort and sometimes violent disruption, quieted.

She held the granite.

She was not the same person who had stood in the Oracle Room four months ago with her signal generator and her measurement microphone and her professional composure. She was not enlightened. She was not saved. She was not in possession of answers the world needed. She was a thirty-eight-year-old acoustic engineer who had learned, slowly and at considerable cost, to be somewhat more present in her own life, and who had decided to use her specific expertise in the service of something that mattered, and who had called her sister twice in the past month, and who was going to keep trying to hold what the floor had shown her with as much honesty as she could manage on any given day, which would vary, which would sometimes fail, which was not a reason not to try.

She thought of all of them. Elisabetta making coffee in Valletta, the cat sleeping in the winter light. Tarık at his government desk with his aquifer depletion figures, still writing the reports, still doing the work. Fatima with her hands on the ancient stone. Tendai at his school, teaching the history that was also the present, selecting his students with the care of someone who understood that the wrong recipient was worse than no recipient at all. Chen Liwei in her laboratory, the EEG plots on the wall, the knowledge she was not yet ready to publish accumulating in notebooks that were already, she had told Mara, more complete than anything she had professionally declared. Rodrigo in his village at altitude, the glaciers retreating in the light. Valentina at her municipal desk, building across the century for the people who would not know her name. Anand in his garden, pressing things into the earth with both hands, angry about two bridges and at peace with everything else.

Nine people on five continents who had never met and had been waiting, each in their place, with the knowledge of a world more complete and more communicative than the one their civilization was building toward, holding it in their bodies and their practices and their particular faithful presences at their particular sites, alone, for years.

She had connected them.

Not to organize them into something. Not to create an institution or a movement or a counter-narrative. Simply to let them know that they were not alone in the way they had been alone, that the fragment each carried was not the whole and that the whole existed, distributed, held across the world in people who had paid the cost of holding it and would keep paying it, because the alternative — releasing it, letting it go, allowing the narration to close fully over the floor — was not something any of them were capable of now that they had found it.

Her laptop chimed.

A reply, from Valletta. Timestamp: four in the morning, Malta time. Elisabetta did not sleep well in winter, she had mentioned this, the cold keeping her at the edge of wakefulness, the old city settling around her in the dark.

The email was six words.

I am calling Turkey in the morning.

Mara read it twice. She felt something move through her that was not quite any of the words she had available — not happiness, not relief, not triumph. Something structural, like a bridge finding its true load. Something that had been waiting to be complete and was now, in the most preliminary and fragile and real of ways, beginning to be.

She closed the laptop.

She turned off the kitchen light.

She went to the window and stood in the dark looking at the city — at the lights of the towers and the distant orange smear of the expressway and above it all the sky, from which Chicago had taken most of the stars but not all of them, a few surviving in the gaps between the light pollution, present for whoever stopped long enough and looked carefully enough to find them.

She found them.

She stood there for a long time, in her apartment in January, in the city she had lived in for a decade and was only now beginning to inhabit, listening to the world speaking in its patient continuous way into the silence she was learning, slowly and imperfectly and without guarantee of success, to keep available.

The sea moved, somewhere. The glacier retreated another fraction of a millimeter. The quarry held its unfinished faces in the Pacific dark. The stone walls of Great Zimbabwe stood in the warm southern night, dry-fitted, mortarless, holding without being held.

Somewhere in Valletta an old woman reached for the phone.
