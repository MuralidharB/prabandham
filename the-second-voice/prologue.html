Prologue

Before the First Word

Before the monuments, the world spoke to itself.

It had been doing this for four and a half billion years without an audience, which had not slowed it down. The basalt cooling in the ocean trenches spoke to the seawater moving over it. The glacier spoke to the valley it was filling, slowly, with the patience of things that do not experience patience as a virtue but simply as their condition. The river spoke to the limestone it was dissolving, one molecule at a time, one century at a time, producing in the dark below the ground the chambers and the passages and the resonant spaces that would one day be found by the kind of creature that had learned to hear and had not yet learned to stop listening.

The world spoke in frequencies. In pressure and release. In the long exhalation of volcanic rifts and the sharp percussion of ice calving from a glacier's face and the low continuous murmur of the ocean moving against the stone edges of the continents that were themselves moving, slowly, in directions that took millions of years to become visible and were happening nonetheless, always happening, the planet conducting its perpetual conversation with itself in registers that required patience of a geological order to receive.

The sky spoke differently. The sky had been speaking longest of all — the light arriving from distances so extreme that the stars it reported had sometimes ceased to exist before their message reached the ground. The sky was an archive of endings that looked, from below, like continuity. It wheeled overhead in its long cycles, the constellations completing their precession across the pole in periods of twenty-six thousand years, the planets tracing their ellipses, the solstice sun returning each year to the same declination with the reliability of something that understood commitment in a way no single living thing could, because it operated outside the scale in which living things experienced commitment as a choice.

Then came the cold.

Not gradually. The ice advanced over millennia and then held for a hundred thousand years, covering a third of the land surface of the earth under sheets that in places reached three kilometers of depth. The sea level dropped a hundred and twenty meters as the water was locked into ice, exposing continental shelves that became grasslands and forests and the routes along which the species that had learned to walk upright moved from one end of the world to the other, following the food, following the warmth, following whatever it is that moves a creature from the place it is to the place it needs to be next, which has never been fully understood and may simply be called life.

They moved through a world that was speaking. They did not always hear it. They were occupied with survival, which has always been the primary argument against listening, and it is not a weak argument. But some of them heard. Some of them stopped in a landscape and attended to what the landscape was doing — the way the cliff face caught the sound of the wind and returned it transformed, the way the cave amplified the breathing of the person inside it until the breathing became the cave's breathing too, until the boundary between the body and the stone was acoustically dissolved and what was inside and what was outside became, briefly, a question without a clear answer.

They painted on those walls. Not to record what they saw, the scholars would later argue — they could see the animals anytime, the animals were everywhere. To make something permanent. To say, in the only medium that outlasted flesh: I was here. I saw this. The world was speaking and I was listening and this is what I heard.

Then came the flood.

Twelve thousand years ago, in a geological instant, the ice began to release. Not slowly. The ice sheets that had held for a hundred thousand years collapsed in pulses — vast quantities of meltwater pouring into the oceans, raising sea levels by meters in decades, drowning the exposed shelves where the grasslands had been, submerging the coasts where the settlements had grown up along the shore in the way of people who had always needed fresh water and the sea's provision simultaneously. Whole landscapes went under. The shallow seas of the continental shelves swallowed forests that had taken thousands of years to grow. The people who had lived on those coasts moved inland and upland and carried their stories with them — stories of the world before the flood, of the lands beneath the water, of the catastrophe that no single human life had been long enough to see coming.

The world had spoken very loudly. It had said: nothing you build on this scale is permanent. It had said: the ground beneath your certainty is always moving. It had said, in the language of risen water and collapsed coastline and the screaming of birds displaced from nesting grounds that no longer existed: pay attention. This is what inattention costs.

Some of them heard.

On a hilltop in what would one day be called southeastern Turkey, in the years after the flood, a group of people stood looking at the landscape. They were not farmers — farming had not yet been invented, and would not be for another thousand years, and when it was invented it would be invented nearby, as if the settling that followed the building had made the cultivation thinkable rather than the other way around. They were hunters and gatherers moving through a post-glacial landscape that was wetter and more various than it would later become, full of the megafauna that the warming was beginning, slowly, to diminish.

They had chosen the hilltop because the hilltop spoke. Not metaphorically. The limestone of the hill had a specific acoustic relationship with the wind that moved across the plateau and the sound it made was not like other sounds and the people who were quiet enough had been stopping here for a long time. They understood, in the way that comes before language and survives the loss of language and cannot be entirely killed by any amount of noise, that this place was doing something. That the world was thinner here. That what was always being said was, at this place and in conditions they had learned to recognize, audible.

They decided to mark it.

Not with a grave, not with a shelter, not with anything that served the needs of the body or the continuity of the lineage in the ordinary sense. They brought stone from a distance. They shaped it with tools whose making had required generations of knowledge. They raised pillars that had no structural purpose — they held nothing up, enclosed nothing, protected nothing. They carved into the stone the animals that moved through their world: the fox, the crane, the aurochs, the snake. Not as trophies. As acknowledgment. As the record of a world that had been attended to and had been found, in the attending, to be more present and more various and more worth the attention than inattention had allowed.

They worked for generations. They did not finish, in the sense of reaching a fixed conclusion, because the work was not that kind of work — it was the kind that extended as far as the attention that sustained it, and the attention, across the generations that labored on the hill, did not diminish but deepened, the later builders understanding what they were doing more completely than the earlier ones had, each generation inheriting not only the technique but the quality of listening that had produced it.

And then they buried it.

Deliberately. Carefully. Filling the enclosures with rubble and sealing them into the earth with the thoroughness of people who had thought about this — who had understood that the world they lived in was changing, that the knowledge of how to be quiet and how to listen and how to mark the places where the boundary was thin was not safe in a world that was becoming louder, that was becoming more occupied with survival in its immediate forms, that was beginning to make the choices that would accumulate into civilization and in doing so accumulate also into the deafness that was civilization's persistent condition.

They put it back into the earth. They said: this is not for now. This is for later. This is for the time when the noise will be at its loudest and the ground will have forgotten what it is speaking and the sky will be obscured and the rivers will have been made to go where they are told and the stone will be mined for gravel and the hilltop will be a tourist site and the world will still be speaking — the world will always be speaking, it has been speaking since before the first cell divided in the first warm water, it will be speaking after the last person is gone — and someone will need to remember that it does.

Someone will need to be quiet enough to hear.

This is not the story of the monuments.

The monuments are older than story. They precede the narrative impulse, which arrived later, when the species had become sufficiently defended against the immediate demands of existence to afford the luxury of sequence and causation and the consoling architecture of beginning, middle, and end. The monuments were built before any of that. They were built when the relationship between the human body and the speaking world was still close enough to be felt without mediation, when the membrane between what was inside and what was outside had not yet been reinforced by the ten thousand years of management that would follow.

They were built as a door held open.

What follows is the story of one person learning to walk through it.

The door has been there all along. The world behind it has not stopped speaking. This is simply the account of a particular silence, in a particular body, at a particular moment in the long history of a species that built extraordinary things to help itself hear — and then, for a time, forgot why.

End of Prologue
