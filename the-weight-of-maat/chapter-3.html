Chapter Three of The Weight of Ma'at — Kha confronts the overseer and learns the conspiracy reaches far beyond the village

The Weight of Ma'at

Chapter Three

The Weight a Man Carries

Deir el-Medina — The Following Morning

"Do not speak to a man in his blindness. Beware of disturbing him with your words. The heart of a man is his own Nile: beware of flooding its banks."

— The Maxims of Ptahhotep, Dynasty V

The overseer's name was Pannehesy, and he had held his position for twenty-two years, which meant he had outlasted three Pharaohs, two viziers, one catastrophic flood, and four attempts by his rival in the right gang to have him removed on grounds that shifted with the political weather but always amounted, at their core, to the same basic complaint: that Pannehesy was careful to the point of paralysis, slow to commit, slow to speak, and constitutionally incapable of the kind of decisive action that a man in his position sometimes needed to take. His critics were not entirely wrong. Panenhesy's caution was legendary in the village — the kind of caution that had kept him employed and relatively safe through twenty-two years of the particular dangers that attended his position, which were the dangers of knowing too much about too many things and being therefore useful to too many people with competing interests.

He was also, Kha had always understood, a fundamentally decent man who had been placed at the centre of something indecent, and who had responded to this placement in the only way his nature allowed, which was to become very still and hope that the thing moved past him.

The thing had not moved past him. And Kha was now standing at his door at the second hour of the morning, before the crews had assembled for the walk to the valley, in the narrow window of time when the street was empty and a conversation could happen without witnesses.

Panenhesy opened the door before Kha knocked. He had been watching from inside — Kha could tell by the way he stood, already composed, already wearing the expression of a man who has been waiting for a specific visitor and has had time to arrange his face. He was a heavyset man in his late fifties, with the broad shoulders of someone who had done physical work for the first decade of his career before advancing to oversight, and the careful eyes of someone who had spent the second decade learning that observation was more valuable than participation. He looked at Kha for a moment without speaking. Then he stepped back from the door and Kha went in.

The house was dim. No lamp lit, though the morning was still grey enough to warrant one. This was information. A man who did not light his lamp was a man who did not want anyone passing outside to know he was awake and receiving visitors.

"I found the western passage," Kha said. He did not sit. He stood in the middle of the room with his hands at his sides, not aggressively, but in the manner of someone who has decided that this particular conversation would be conducted standing because sitting implied a comfort neither of them currently possessed. "The plaster is new. Someone has been through it recently."

Panenhesy said nothing. He moved to the far wall and stood against it, arms crossed over his chest, in the posture of a man who has made himself into a smaller target.

"I also," Kha said, "spoke last night with the scribe's assistant. Nefertari-Tawy. Who is currently in possession of Hapu's inventory records and his access credentials, and who has identified at least six tombs in which burial goods have been systematically removed and the records falsified." He paused. "And who has found your name in the inspection authorizations."

This was not entirely true. Nefertari had found an authorization code that traced back to the overseer's administrative seal — she had told him this the night before, in the last ten minutes of their conversation, when the lamp was guttering and her voice had gone flat with exhaustion. She had not said the overseer's name. But an authorization code and a name were, in this context, the same thing, and Kha had decided that the distinction was a courtesy Panenhesy had not earned.

The silence in the room had texture. It was the silence of a man calculating — not whether he had been found out, because that calculation was already complete, but what finding-out meant for the remaining variables. How much she knew. How much Kha knew. Whether the gap between what they knew and what could be proven was still wide enough to matter.

"You should be careful," Panenhesy said finally. His voice was very quiet. Not threatening — or not only threatening. It was the quietness of genuine warning from a man who understood the terrain. "Both of you."

"I know," Kha said. "Hapu was careful too."

The name landed. Panenhesy's face did not change, but something behind it did — a small movement, deep, the way a building settles when something shifts in its foundation. He uncrossed his arms and crossed them again. He looked at the unlit lamp on the table as though it had become interesting.

"Hapu," he said, "asked questions in the wrong places."

"Hapu," Kha said, "fell thirty feet in the dark."

"Yes."

"And Userhat."

The second name hit differently than the first. Panenhesy went very still — the particular stillness of a man who has been carrying something heavy for a long time and has just had the weight shifted onto a different part of his body. He looked at Kha. His eyes were red at the edges, and Kha understood with a cold, specific clarity that this was not new redness — it was old. The redness of a man who had not slept well in years.

"Userhat asked his question aloud," Panenhesy said. It came out like a confession — not the legal kind, but the kind a man made to himself in the small hours when the accounting of his choices caught up with him. "You did not."

"No," Kha said. "I did not."

"That is the only reason —" Panenhesy stopped. He pressed his lips together. He started again. "I want you to understand that I did not know, at the time, what would happen to Userhat. I was told the passage was necessary. I was told it was a construction provision. By the time I understood what it was actually for, Userhat was already —" He stopped again. The sentence had a place to go but he was not going to take it there.

"You gave the order," Kha said. "The passage was cut on your authority."

"Yes."

"You signed the inspection authorizations for the other tombs."

"I signed documents that I was told were routine administrative procedure. I did not —" He made a sound that was not quite a word, something between frustration and self-contempt. "I am not a stupid man, Kha. I knew they were not routine. I told myself that what I did not ask about, I did not know. And what I did not know, I could not be held responsible for."

"And the objects? The canopic jars, the ushabti —"

"I did not know about the objects." He said it with a force that surprised Kha — not the force of a man insisting on his innocence, but the force of a man who has been carrying the specific horror of a thing he genuinely did not anticipate and cannot stop thinking about. "I thought it was information. I thought someone was using the tombs as — as a place to store documents. Communications. Things they did not want found. Political things. I told myself it was political. That it was between powerful men and their disputes and that I was a very small mechanism in a very large system and that none of it had anything to do with the dead."

He stopped. The word dead sat in the unlighted room and neither of them addressed it for a moment.

"Who ordered the passage?" Kha asked.

Panenhesy looked at him. His face had the expression of a man at the edge of something he has been backing away from for years and has finally run out of ground behind him.

"If I tell you that," he said, "I cannot protect you."

"You are not currently protecting me."

"I am, in fact." The quiet force returned to his voice, and under it something that sounded almost like grief. "The fact that you are standing in my house asking me questions instead of at the bottom of a shaft is because I have been protecting you. Your name has come up. More than once. You are the foreman who cut the passage, and you were Userhat's closest colleague, and there are people who believe you know more than you have said. I have told them, each time, that you are a careful man who understands the value of silence, and that you are therefore not a problem." He looked at Kha steadily. "You are currently in the process of becoming a problem."

Kha took this in. He thought about his daughter asleep on her mat. He thought about the sand in her sandals and the answer that had been too quick and too smooth, the answer of someone who had been told what to say. Someone had reached into his house, past him, and put a hand on Merit, and had done it so gently that he had not noticed until now, standing in a dim room being told that his continued survival was contingent on his continued blindness.

"My daughter was taken into the valley," Kha said. His voice came out level, which surprised him. "At night. By someone from this village."

Panenhesy's expression did not change. This was itself an answer, and they both understood it.

"She was shown something," Kha said. "She was shown something and told to call it a game, and she called it a game, and I believed her because I was choosing to believe her, and whoever brought her there knew I would choose to believe her because I am a careful man who understands the value of silence." He paused. "That is not protection. That is leverage."

The word landed in the room and did not leave it. Panenhesy looked at the floor. He was not a man capable of the complete absence of conscience — this was, Kha thought, both his fundamental decency and his fundamental uselessness in the current situation. A man with no conscience would have been easier to predict. A man with a conscience that he had spent twenty-two years overriding was a much more complicated instrument.

"Tell me who ordered the passage," Kha said. "Not for the record. Not for the scribe's documents. Tell me so I know the shape of what I am standing inside."

Panenhesy told him.

He told him in the way that people told things they had been holding for too long — not all at once, not in a clear organized account, but in fragments, each one released with the specific reluctance of a man dismantling a wall he built for his own protection and understanding, as each stone came out, exactly how load-bearing it had been.

The order for the passage had come from the office of the Mayor of Thebes. Not directly — not in writing, not under the Mayor's own seal. It had come through an intermediary, a temple administrator from Karnak who visited the village three times a year on legitimate treasury business and who on one particular visit, five years after the current tomb's construction had begun, had taken Panenhesy aside during the evening meal and spoken to him quietly about an arrangement that would benefit everyone involved and harm no one who did not deserve to be harmed. The passage was presented as a contingency. The inspection authorizations were presented as a precaution. The money — and there had been money, Panenhesy said this looking at the floor, not at Kha, a yearly payment deposited in grain and oil to a secondary household account that his wife believed was a performance bonus from the necropolis administration — the money was presented as recognition.

"The Mayor of Thebes," Kha said.

"The Mayor's office," Panenhesy said, with the precision of a man who had spent years maintaining a very particular distinction. "I do not know if the Mayor himself —"

"Does the distinction matter?"

Panenhesy was quiet.

"The temple administrator," Kha said. "His name."

"Nebwenenef." He said it quickly, as though speed made it a smaller thing. "He is the third accountant in the treasury office of the temple of Amun at Karnak. A minor official. Unremarkable. He has been in that position for eleven years and he will be in it for eleven more and no one will ever look at him closely unless they already know what they are looking for."

"He is the mechanism," Kha said. "The false inspection codes — they come from his section of the records."

Panenhesy looked at him sharply. "How do you know that?"

"The scribe's assistant is thorough," Kha said. "She is also going to run out of time inside Hapu's access credentials within the week, which means that whatever she is going to find, she needs to find now, and whatever she finds will need to go somewhere it cannot be suppressed." He looked at the overseer steadily. "Who above the Mayor?"

"I don't know."

"Panenhesy."

"I genuinely do not know." The genuine was weighted — the word a man used when he needed you to understand that this particular statement was not in the same category as the other statements, which had been careful and partial and shaped by self-interest. "Nebwenenef does not tell me. The Mayor is — the Mayor is a consequence, not an origin. He is being paid, or being protected, or being threatened, and in any of those cases he is doing what he is told by someone whose name I have never heard and whose face I have never seen. Someone with the authority to alter documents at the level of the Vizier's administrative archive." He looked at Kha with something that was close to fear, but was more specific than fear. It was the expression of a man who has spent years not looking at something and has now been made to look at it in full light. "This is not a village problem, Kha. It is not even a Theban problem. Whatever is above the Mayor is above the Mayor by a very considerable distance."

The room was silent. Somewhere in the street outside, the first sounds of the village waking — a door, a child's voice, the rhythm of someone grinding grain. Normal sounds. The sounds of a morning that did not know what was happening inside this particular house.

"Userhat," Kha said. He said it the way you returned to a subject after you had followed all the branches and needed to come back to the root. "He was at the briefing. He questioned the passage. And then he died."

"I was told it was precautionary," Panenhesy said. His voice had gone to almost nothing. "I was told he had spoken to people outside the village. That he had mentioned the passage to a man on the east bank ferry. That it was a risk that needed to be managed. I was not told —" He stopped. He pressed the heels of both hands against his eyes, the gesture of a man trying to push back something that is pressing forward from inside. "I was not told. I want you to understand that the word they used was managed. I told myself managed meant — that they would speak to him. That they would warn him. That managed meant something that was not —"

"A shaft," Kha said.

"Yes." The word came out as a breath. "A shaft."

Kha stood in the half-dark and looked at the overseer — a man in his late fifties who had built his professional life on caution and had watched that caution become, over twenty-two years of small concessions, indistinguishable from complicity. He did not feel contempt for him. He had expected to feel contempt, had prepared for it on the walk over, had thought he would need it as a kind of armor. What he felt instead was something worse, which was recognition. The difference between Panenhesy and himself was not a difference of character. It was a difference of what they had been asked, and when, and how the asking had been framed, and which questions they had allowed themselves to pursue and which they had put on the shelf in the dark and left there. Kha had put Userhat on the shelf. He had put the passage on the shelf. He had, for seven years, been a careful man who understood the value of silence, and the silence had cost him nothing that he could see, which was the most dangerous possible way to pay for silence because it meant you kept paying without feeling the weight of it.

Until now.

"I need the name of the merchant," Kha said. "The one on the east bank. The one who is receiving the objects."

"If I give you that —"

"You are already in this," Kha said. Not harshly. It was simply the true thing, said clearly. "There is no action you can take now that removes you from it. The only remaining question is what kind of role you play, and whether that role is the kind that survives the accounting." He did not say the accounting of Ma'at — he did not need to. In this village, in this context, between two men who had spent their entire lives in the service of a civilization built on the principle that every action had a consequence that outlasted death, the phrase did not require elaboration.

Panenhesy looked at him for a long moment. Then he went to a small cedar chest in the corner of the room — the kind that held personal documents, deed records, land-grant certificates — and from beneath a folded linen wrapping he produced a small ostracon, a pottery shard with writing on it in hieratic script. He held it for a moment before he gave it over, the way a man held a thing before releasing it when he understood the releasing was irreversible.

"Nebwenenef's contact on the east bank," he said. "A merchant named Amenwia, near the processional avenue of the temple of Amun. He moves the objects through a network of private buyers. Wealthy families, collectors, people who ask no questions because asking questions would complicate a purchase they have already decided to make." He held out the shard. "The merchant does not know who is above Nebwenenef. No one below the Mayor does. This is by design."

Kha took the ostracon. It was warm from being held.

"There is something else," Panenhesy said. He said it with the quality of a man who has been building up to a particular thing and has been delaying it and can no longer justify the delay. "The most recent entry in the western passage — the one you found yesterday — was not a removal. I mean to say, they took something. But they also left something."

Kha went very still. "What did they leave?"

"I don't know precisely. Nebwenenef told me last month that a particular item needed to be placed in the chamber for a specific period — he called it a storage arrangement, which is the kind of language he uses when he does not want to be precise. He said it was temporary. He said it was not connected to the removal operation." Panenhesy looked at him with the eyes of a man who no longer believed the things he had been told but had not known, until this moment, how to say so. "He said it would not affect the tomb. That it was simply being kept there for safekeeping."

"Safekeeping," Kha said.

"Yes."

"In a royal burial chamber."

"Yes."

"Something that needed to be in a royal burial chamber for safekeeping."

He did not say what they were both thinking, which was that there was only one category of object that benefited from being kept in proximity to a royal burial — that drew on the accumulated heka of a site consecrated by decades of ritual preparation, charged by the names and prayers of a thousand craftsmen, sealed with the authority of the Pharaoh's own funerary rites — and that category of object was not being stored. It was being fed. It was being kept in the most concentrated source of sacred power in the western bank and left there to absorb it, the way you left bread near an oven not to store it but to make it rise.

The cold that had begun in his spine the day before extended itself upward, slowly and with great precision, vertebra by vertebra.

"When was it placed?" Kha asked.

"Three weeks ago."

"And when was it removed?"

"Yesterday," Panenhesy said. "According to the arrangement."

Yesterday. The fresh plaster. The displaced dust. The thing that had been in the burial chamber had been removed on the exact day Kha had found the evidence of its removal. The day he had gone to inspect the measurements. The day that, had he arrived an hour earlier, he might have encountered whoever had come to collect it.

"Did it go to the merchant?" Kha asked.

"I don't know. I was not told. I was told only that the arrangement was complete and that the passage would not be used again." He paused. "I was also told, in the same message, that the current audit — Hapu's audit, and now by extension the woman's audit — would be concluded within the week."

The word concluded sat in the room and defined its own meaning without assistance.

Kha left the overseer's house at the third hour, when the street was filling with the morning movement of the village — crews assembling, children being sent on errands, women at the communal ovens, the whole ordinary machinery of a day that did not know it was different from any other day. He walked through it without speaking to anyone, because his face would not have been useful in conversation and he knew it.

He went directly to the scribe's house.

Nefertari had been awake since before dawn — he could tell by the amount of work on the table, which had nearly doubled overnight, and by the quality of her stillness, which was no longer the controlled stillness of the evening before but the slightly frayed stillness of someone running on will rather than rest. She looked at him when he came in and something in his face told her immediately that the overseer's visit had gone beyond what she had anticipated, because she set down her pen without being asked and gave him her full attention.

He told her everything. He told it precisely and without omission, including the part about Merit, which he had not planned to include but which came out anyway because it was relevant and because the effort of not saying it was one he could not currently afford. She listened without interrupting, which was a form of respect he noted. When he finished, she was quiet for a moment, looking at the papyrus sheets on the table.

"The object in the chamber," she said. "The one that was placed there three weeks ago and removed yesterday."

"Yes."

"Nebwenenef is a temple accountant. His access is administrative. He could not place a charged object in a royal tomb on his own authority — the ritual requirements alone would require someone with genuine priestly training. Someone who knew the correct procedures for introducing an object into a consecrated space without triggering the tomb's protective mechanisms."

"The hekau," Kha said. The protective spells. Every royal tomb was sealed with them — words of power inscribed at specific junctions in the architecture, designed to make the space hostile to unauthorized presence. Kha's crew worked around them; they were as much a part of the construction specification as the drainage channels. A layperson walking into a protected chamber uninvited would not immediately die, because the spells were not crude mechanisms. But an object placed in such a space without the correct counter-ritual would not absorb the chamber's power. It would be rejected by it, or worse, corrupted by the collision.

"Someone with genuine ritual knowledge performed the placement," Nefertari said. "Which means either a trained priest was physically in that chamber, or —" She stopped. She picked up one of the papyrus sheets and looked at it for a moment. "Three months ago, I processed a request, as part of the routine audit, for the necropolis records of a priest of Sokar."

Kha looked at her. Sokar was the funerary god of the Memphis necropolis — ancient, predating even Osiris in some traditions, the god of the dead in their most physical form, the god of the sealed chamber, the god of the space between death and resurrection. His priesthood was small and specialized and carried a level of ritual authority over the physical spaces of the dead that almost no other priestly order could match.

"His name," Kha said.

"Usermontu." She said it carefully, as though the name itself required handling. "He has been stationed at the mortuary temple of Ramesses III for the last seven years. His official duties are the maintenance of the daily offering rites for the temple's resident dead. His unofficial movements —" She turned the papyrus sheet toward him. "Are not in the official record at all, which is itself interesting, because a priest of his standing should have a movement record. Visits to other temples, festival duties, administrative consultations. The record exists up to seven years ago and then it becomes very thin. Just enough entries to confirm he is alive and employed. Nothing more."

"Seven years," Kha said.

"Yes."

Seven years. Userhat's death. The construction of the hidden passage. The beginning of the inspection falsifications. All of it beginning at the same point, spreading outward from the same root, and at the root — not a mayor, not a merchant, not a treasury accountant — a priest with the ritual knowledge to walk into a royal tomb and do something to it that a layperson could not do. Something that required the specific authority of a man whose entire vocation was the management of the boundary between the living and the dead.

"What was he doing in the chamber?" Kha asked. He meant it generally — not what he was physically doing, but the larger question, the one that the cold in his spine had been building toward since yesterday. What did you need to keep for three weeks in the most ritually charged space on the west bank? What required that kind of power, accumulated over that length of time, and then needed to be removed on a specific day?

Nefertari set the papyrus sheet down. She looked at him with the expression of someone who has arrived at a conclusion they would prefer not to share because sharing it makes it more real.

"The canopic jars," she said slowly. "The ushabti. The heart scarabs. They are not being sold as antiques." She stopped. Started again. "Or rather, they are being sold as antiques. That is the mechanism. That is how the money moves and how the buyers are insulated from understanding what they have actually purchased. But the objects themselves — the specific objects, taken from specific tombs in a specific order —" She looked at the inventory list. "This is a collection. This is someone assembling something. The objects are not random. They have been selected."

The room was very still.

"Selected for what?" Kha asked.

"I don't know yet." She met his eyes. "But I know the selection is not complete. There are three tombs in the valley whose records show the same false inspection code as the others, whose goods have not yet been touched. Either the operation was interrupted by Hapu's investigation, or —" She paused. "Or those tombs are being left for last. Because what comes from them is what everything else has been building toward."

Kha looked at the inventory list. He looked at the three tomb designations she had marked at the bottom of the sheet — three royal tombs, three sets of burial goods still in place, three rooms in the valley that had been targeted and not yet touched. He recognized all three designations. He had worked in two of them during his early career, as a young cutter under a previous foreman. He knew their layouts, their depths, their protective inscriptions. He knew which one contained the most extensive and precisely executed set of funerary equipment in the valley. The one that, if you were assembling something that required the power of royal death, you would save for last.

He straightened up. He looked at Nefertari. She was already looking back at him with the expression of someone who has had the same thought and is waiting to confirm it is shared.

"We need to know what was in that chamber," he said. "What Usermontu placed there and removed yesterday. Before it reaches whoever it is being taken to."

"The merchant's contact on the east bank," she said.

"Yes. But that is a trail with an end we cannot yet see." He picked up the ostracon Panenhesy had given him and set it on the table beside her papyrus sheets, where it joined the growing record of something that was far larger and far older than a village robbery scandal. "We need to go to the mortuary temple of Ramesses III. We need to find Usermontu before he is told that the audit is about to be concluded."

The word concluded had followed him from the overseer's house and it sat with them now in the lamplight, patient and final.

"That is outside the village," Nefertari said. Not an objection — an observation, made in the manner of someone cataloguing the risks of a course of action they have already decided to take. The village of Deir el-Medina had rules about movement. Crew members did not leave without authorization during the working week. A foreman disappearing without leave would be noticed, reported, investigated.

"I have not taken authorized leave in three years," Kha said. "I am owed eleven days."

"And I have Hapu's movement authorization, which covers the mortuary temples in my inspection remit." She paused. "Which expires in five days along with everything else."

"Five days," Kha said.

"Five days."

Five days to find a priest who had performed a forbidden ritual in a royal tomb. Five days before the administrative machinery that had been keeping them both technically legitimate ran out. Five days before whoever had concluded Hapu concluded them.

And Merit, asleep in their house at the south end of the village, who had sand in her sandals from a place she should not have been, and who had not been sleeping well, and at whose door Kha had returned the Bes figurine to its corner the night before with the specific, narrow prayer of a father who has finally understood what he should have been afraid of all along.

He would need to deal with that before he left. He would need to deal with what to tell her, and where to put her, and who could be trusted in this village with the specific task of keeping his daughter safe when he was not here to do it himself — which was a question with a very short list of answers and several of them, he suspected, were already on the wrong side of this.

He picked up the ostracon and put it in his belt. He looked around the dead scribe's house — the papyrus sheets, the ink-stained mat, the cedar box, the cold cup of water — and he thought about Hapu at twenty-four, thorough and ambitious and dead at the bottom of a shaft, and about Userhat, who had asked his question aloud, and about all the questions Kha had asked only inwardly for thirty years, and he thought that the shelf in the dark where he had kept them was now empty. Everything on it had come down. The only remaining question was what to do with the weight of it.

"Tomorrow morning," he said. "Before the crews assemble. We leave from the south gate, not the north, because the north gate keeper reports to the overseer's office and the south gate keeper has been asleep at his post since the spring festival and I have never reported it."

Nefertari looked at him. A small thing crossed her face — not quite a smile, because the situation did not warrant a smile, but its structural predecessor. The thing that lives in the face before a smile arrives. "You are more useful than I expected," she said.

"I am a foreman," Kha said. "I know every weakness in every wall I have ever built." He moved toward the door. "Get some sleep. You look like a person who has been awake since the previous dynasty."

He went out into the street. The village was fully alive around him now — the morning in complete motion, the bright ordinary noise of people going about the business of a day that was, for all of them except two, exactly like every other day. He walked south through it, past the ovens and the water carriers and the children who moved out of the way of a foreman on his morning rounds without being asked, and he thought about what Panenhesy had said. Whatever is above the Mayor is above the Mayor by a very considerable distance.

He thought about what Nefertari had said. The objects have been selected. This is someone assembling something.

He thought about the object that had spent three weeks in the burial chamber of a living Pharaoh, drinking in the accumulated heka of the most ritually prepared space on the west bank of the Nile, and had been removed yesterday, and was now somewhere on the east bank moving toward whoever had sent it there to be fed.

He thought: we are already late.

Then he went home to see his daughter, and to sit with her over the morning bread, and to look at her face — her mother's face, the face he had been carrying for six years like a stone he would not put down — and to say nothing of any of this, and to do it so convincingly that she would not know, from his expression, that anything had changed.

He was a foreman. He had been keeping things from his face for twenty-six years. He was very good at it.

He was less certain, this particular morning, that it was still a skill worth having.
