Chapter Four

The River Between Worlds

The Nile Crossing — Early Morning, Day Two

"The Nile is in the sky, it is in the earth, it is on the mountains. It does not know weariness. It sees what it wishes to see. It hears what it wishes to hear."

— Hymn to the Nile, Middle Kingdom

The Nile in the early morning was a different river from the one it became by midday. At the hour before full sunrise it was grey and absolutely still, the color of old pewter, and it held the reflections of the western cliffs so precisely that you could stand at the ferry landing and believe, for a moment, that the world had been doubled and placed face to face with itself — the cliffs above and the cliffs below, the living bank and the dead bank, the papyrus reeds at the water's edge and their perfect inverted counterparts reaching downward into a sky that was also a river. The boatman who ferried the necropolis workers back and forth had been making this crossing for thirty years and had long since stopped seeing any of it, which Kha had always considered one of the small tragedies of familiarity.

He stood at the bow and looked east. Thebes was waking on the far bank — the smudge of cookfire smoke rising in thin columns above the roofline, the white facades of the temple of Amun beginning to catch the first oblique light, the processional avenue of sphinxes that led from the river landing to the great entrance pylon visible even from this distance as a dark geometric interruption in the pale morning. He had crossed this river more times than he could count, for festival days and official business and the occasional domestic errand, and it had never once felt like a neutral act. The Nile was a threshold. Everyone on the west bank knew it. You lived in the shadow of the dead, and the river was the boundary between their world and the world of the living, and every crossing was a small negotiation with that boundary — a reminder that you were, for the moment, on the permitted side.

Nefertari stood at the stern with her document satchel across her shoulder and her eyes on the east bank, already cataloguing. She had been awake before him — he had arrived at the south gate at the agreed time to find her already there, already organized, already carrying everything she needed in a bundle the size of a market basket, which suggested either excellent efficiency or that she had not slept at all. He had not asked which.

They had not spoken much on the walk from the village to the landing. The pre-dawn dark had a quality that discouraged conversation — not hostility, but the mutual understanding that the time for planning had passed and the time for doing had begun, and that talking was a way of stalling, and neither of them could afford to stall. He had told Merit, the evening before, that he had authorized leave for an inspection consultation on the east bank and would be gone two days at most. He had told it simply, without elaboration, and Merit had looked at him across the bread with the particular look of a seventeen-year-old who suspects she is not being told the whole truth and has calculated that pressing the matter will result in less information rather than more. She had said: be careful. He had said: I am always careful. She had said nothing, which was its own answer.

He had also, before leaving, visited the house of the painter Nebnefer — the oldest man in Deir el-Medina, a widower whose children were grown and who spent his retirement years doing small repair work on older tomb paintings when his eyes allowed and sitting in his doorway watching the street when they did not. Nebnefer was the most trustworthy person Kha knew, in the specific sense that he was too old and too tired to have interests that competed with honesty, and he had agreed, without asking for a reason, to look in on Merit twice a day. He had done this in the past, in the years after Kha's wife died, when Merit was eleven and the village had collectively and quietly organized itself around the task of ensuring she did not fall through the gaps. This was one of the things about Deir el-Medina that was not complicated or dark — the way it closed around its own in difficulty, the way a community did when it was small enough that every loss was personal and every child was everyone's child in some working sense.

He was grateful for it this morning in a way that had more weight than usual.

The east bank received them with the specific indifference of a large city to all arrivals. The ferry landing at Thebes was already busy at this hour — farmers bringing produce in from the delta settlements, merchants with loaded donkeys, temple workers in their white linen moving with the purposeful unhurriedness of people whose working day was governed by ritual schedules rather than practical ones. Nobody looked at a foreman and a scribe's assistant stepping off the morning ferry. Nobody looked at anything on the Theban landing except their own destination.

This was the first thing that struck Kha about the east bank, every time he came. The anonymity of it. In Deir el-Medina, your face was known before you opened your mouth. Your history walked with you. Your grandfather's name was part of your name. Here, on the east bank, in the teeming ordinary complexity of a city that held more people in a single district than his entire village had ever held in its whole history, you were no one in particular unless you made yourself someone, and the freedom of that was matched precisely by its danger — because no one who meant you harm needed to explain himself either.

They walked south along the processional avenue, staying close to the edge where the sphinx statues gave some shade against the early sun. The avenue was lined on both sides with the ram-headed sphinxes of Amun, each one identical in its carved composure, each one cradling between its forepaws a small figure of the Pharaoh in the posture of supplication. Kha had seen these statues a hundred times and had always found them faintly unsettling in a way he could not quite name — something about the repetition, the way the same face multiplied endlessly in both directions created the impression not of a procession but of a single moment endlessly reflected, as though time itself had become stuck at this precise point along this precise road.

"The mortuary temple of Ramesses III is at Medinet Habu," Nefertari said, falling into step beside him. "On the west bank, technically. We are not going there first."

"No."

"You want the merchant."

"I want to know what left the burial chamber before we confront the man who put it there." He kept his voice level, at the pitch of a professional conversation between two people on legitimate business. "If Usermontu knows we are coming — and we must assume he will be warned, because the network that has been running this operation for seven years does not leave its priests unprotected — then he will say as little as possible, and what little he says will be shaped by what he believes we already know. The merchant is the object end of this. He can tell us what was collected yesterday. What form it took. What it was being used for."

"He will not tell us voluntarily."

"No," Kha agreed. "But I have Hapu's inventory list in your bag with the merchant's name on it, and you have a necropolis audit authority stamp that is still technically valid, and a merchant who has been quietly receiving stolen burial goods for fourteen months is a man with a very strong interest in the difference between an official inquiry and an unofficial one." He looked at her. "We are not going to threaten him. We are going to give him the opportunity to be the person who cooperated rather than the person who obstructed, and we are going to make that opportunity seem extremely time-limited."

Nefertari was quiet for a moment. Then: "You have done this before."

"I have managed workmen for twenty-six years," Kha said. "The techniques are transferable."

The merchant Amenwia operated from a house near the southern end of the processional avenue that was larger than it appeared from the street and smaller than it appeared from inside — a quality achieved by the specific arrangement of storage rooms and display chambers that characterized the homes of men who sold things they preferred not to advertise. The front room was entirely respectable: bolts of good Nubian cloth, small alabaster cosmetic vessels, a selection of amulets that were the kind of quality you bought for living relatives rather than the funerary grade you commissioned for yourself. The back rooms, separated by a heavy curtain of dyed wool, were where the more considered purchases were made.

He was a man of perhaps fifty, compact and watchful, with the careful eyes of a professional evaluator — a man who had spent his working life assessing the value of objects and had extended this practice, inevitably, to people. He assessed Kha and Nefertari in the three seconds between the door opening and the door closing, and what he saw made him recalibrate twice in quick succession: first when he registered Nefertari's document satchel and the particular quality of her linen, which was House of Life grade; and second when he saw the stamp she placed on his counter without preamble, which was the necropolis audit authority stamp, which he would not have seen before but which he would recognize, as any man in his position would recognize it, as the specific thing he had spent fourteen months hoping never to see.

"I have all my trading licenses," he said immediately. This was the response of a man who had prepared a response.

"I am certain you do," Nefertari said. Her voice had changed — not cold, not aggressive, but carrying the impersonal authority of someone for whom paperwork was a weapon she had been trained to use. "The licenses are not what interests me. What interests me is a delivery you received yesterday, from a contact in the necropolis workers' community, consisting of a single object of an unusual type. I have the catalogue number. I would like to see the object."

The merchant looked at Kha. Kha looked back at him with the expression of a man who is present in a professional capacity and has no personal feelings about the outcome, which was not entirely true but was useful.

"I receive many objects," Amenwia said carefully.

"You receive stolen burial goods from the royal necropolis and you sell them to wealthy Theban families who want antique funerary equipment without paying for commissioned work." Nefertari said it with the same tone she might have used to read a balance sheet — not accusatory, not theatrical. Simply the enumeration of established facts. "The families are your larger problem, not mine. What I need from you, today, is the object from yesterday's delivery, and the name of the person who delivered it. In exchange for which, you and your current inventory do not appear in the report I file with the Vizier's office at the end of this week." She paused. "I will be filing a report regardless. The question is whether your name is in it as a source of assistance or as a subject of investigation."

The merchant looked at the stamp. He looked at the satchel. He looked at the curtain to the back room and then back at Nefertari, and in his face Kha could see the precise calculation of a man weighing two kinds of danger against each other — the danger of talking and the danger of staying silent — and finding, as people generally did when the calculation was laid out this plainly, that one of them was considerably larger than the other.

"The delivery yesterday," he said slowly, "was not a normal piece."

"No," Nefertari said. "I did not imagine it was."

"The man who brought it — I do not know his name. He comes infrequently. He is a priest, or was — he has the bearing of it, the shaved head, the posture. But he does not wear temple whites. He dresses as a craftsman, or tries to." He moved toward the curtain. "I have not sold the piece. I was told not to sell it. I was told to hold it for collection by a separate party, which is — unusual, in my experience. Most items come through me in one direction only." He held the curtain aside and looked back at them with the expression of a man who has decided that the most dangerous thing in his immediate vicinity is the object in his back room, and that the people asking about it are, comparatively, the lesser problem. "You should come and see it. I want it out of my house."

The back room was dim and smelled of cedar and natron and the faint mineral sweetness of old stone — the smell of things that had been underground for a long time and carried the underground in them. Shelves ran along three walls, crowded with objects in various states of display and storage: canopic jars with their painted stoppers, ushabti figurines in rows, heart scarabs of faience and carnelian and green schist, small wooden models of boats and granaries and the whole miniature infrastructure of the Egyptian afterlife. Kha looked at them with the eye of a man who had spent his career in the service of such objects — knew their purpose, knew their quality, could tell fine work from expedient work at a glance — and felt the compound revulsion of a craftsman seeing his life's work in a place it should not be.

These were not reproductions. These were real. He could see it in the pigment work, in the quality of the stone, in the particular style of the hieroglyphs on several of the larger pieces — hieroglyphs that identified their owners by name and epithet, that specified in the most fundamental sense the person they had been made for and the purpose they were meant to serve. One of them, a canopic jar stoppered with the head of the falcon-god Qebehsenuef, bore an inscription he recognized — not because he had worked in that tomb personally, but because he had seen the design in the construction records during a routine cross-reference three years ago. He knew whose organs that jar was meant to hold. He knew the name inscribed on its base. The name was more than two hundred years old.

He looked away from the shelves.

In the far corner of the room, on a cedar stand that had been hastily cleared of its previous occupant, was the piece from yesterday's delivery.

It was a shabti. A funerary figurine — a small servant-substitute, the kind placed in tombs in their dozens and hundreds so that the dead would have laborers to perform any work the gods of the afterlife might require of them. Every wealthy burial contained them. The better burials contained hundreds, one for each day of the year plus overseers to manage them, a miniature workforce of fired faience or carved stone or molded clay, each one inscribed with the sixth chapter of the Book of the Dead: If I am called, if I am designated to do any work that has to be done in the realm of the dead — you shall say: here I am.

This one was different.

It was approximately two feet tall, which was far larger than any shabti Kha had ever seen in a working context — the standard figurines were hand-sized, small enough to pack in their hundreds into a wooden box. This one was carved from a single piece of black granite with a precision that spoke of a master craftsman at the height of his power, every surface finished to a smoothness that seemed to reject the dim light of the room rather than absorb it. The face was royal — the nemes headdress, the false beard, the serene formality of divine portraiture — but the inscription on its base was wrong. Not wrong in quality. Wrong in content.

Nefertari crossed to it and crouched to read the base inscription. She was silent for a long moment. Then she said, very quietly: "This is not a servant."

"No," Kha said. He had seen it from across the room — the inscription was not the standard shabti formula, not the sixth chapter with its offer of labor. What was inscribed on the base of this figure was something much older and much less common, a text he had seen only once before, on a very early Dynasty tomb wall that his crew had briefly worked near during a drainage project, and which the senior priest who consulted on the project had ordered photographed and covered again without explanation. He had looked it up afterward, in the village's collection of copied religious texts, and found only a partial reference and the notation that the full text was kept in the restricted archive of the House of Life at Heliopolis.

It was a binding inscription. Not a servant formula but a container formula — the kind of text used not to animate an object into service but to seal something inside it. The kind of inscription you placed on a vessel not to send it out into the world but to keep something in.

"It was in the burial chamber for three weeks," Kha said. His voice was level. He was working to keep it level. "Being fed on the heka of a royal tomb. On the accumulated power of a consecrated space." He looked at the granite figure — at the black stone that seemed to pull light toward it without quite returning it. "What is inside it?"

"I cannot read all of it," Nefertari said. She had her face close to the base, following the inscription with her finger without touching it. "The text style is very early — Second Intermediate Period at the latest, possibly older. My training covers the New Kingdom texts primarily." She paused. "But the central determinative — the hieroglyph that classifies the noun —" She stopped. She stood up, slowly, and turned to look at him. Her face had the expression of someone who has just had an academic problem become a personal one. "It is the determinative for sheut."

The shadow. The fifth part of the soul.

Kha stood very still and looked at the granite figure and thought about what it meant to seal a shadow into a stone vessel and then feed the stone vessel on three weeks of royal funerary power. The shadow of the Egyptian understanding was not a metaphor — it was a real and functional component of the soul, the part that followed a person everywhere, that persisted after death, that could be used by those with the knowledge to use it. A shadow separated from its owner was a weapon. A shadow stored and fed and strengthened was something else entirely — something that the restricted archive of the House of Life at Heliopolis apparently felt strongly enough about to keep away from general scholarship.

"Whose shadow?" he asked.

"I don't know. The name on the base has been deliberately removed — there is a section of the inscription where the surface has been re-cut. Carefully. It took someone who knew what they were doing." She looked at the merchant, who was standing near the curtain with his arms crossed over his chest and the expression of a man who has already decided that whatever is discussed in his back room this morning is not something he will be recounting to anyone. "Who is collecting this? The separate party you mentioned."

"I was given a name," Amenwia said. "A house address near the temple of Amun, north quarter. The collection was scheduled for this afternoon." He was talking quickly now, with the fluency of a man who has decided that information is the price of his continued uninvolvement and is paying it readily. "The name on the collection order is a woman's name. Tamyt. She has a title — she is listed as a temple singer of Amun, which is a respectable position, which is why I did not question —"

"A temple singer," Nefertari said. She said it with a flatness that told Kha she had encountered this before in her work — the respectable title used as cover for something that required access to the temple precinct without arousing the notice that a priest would arouse. A temple singer moved freely between the public and sacred spaces of the temple. She attended rituals, carried offerings, maintained the musical dimension of the daily worship schedule. She was, in the architecture of the temple's social order, invisible in the particular way of people who were too present to be remarkable.

"Write down the address," Kha said to the merchant. He set the ostracon from Panenhesy on the merchant's cleared cedar stand. "On the back of this. And then we will take the figure, and you will close your back room for the remainder of the day, and when the collection party comes this afternoon you will tell them there has been a delay in the delivery and you will send word to —" He looked at Nefertari.

"The temporary office of the necropolis audit at the ferry landing," she said. She produced a small papyrus card from her satchel and placed it beside the ostracon. This was, Kha recognized, either a genuine temporary office or a plausible address she had prepared in advance, and he chose not to ask which because both were equally useful for the current purpose.

"You will send word there," Kha said, "the moment they arrive, and you will give them nothing, and you will not mention that you have spoken to us. If you do these things, the report filed at the end of the week will not include your name in connection with the restricted items." He paused. "If you do not do these things, the report will include your name in connection with everything in your back room, and the names of every buyer you have sold to in the last fourteen months, and the specific tomb designations of every object they purchased, and I will personally ensure that the priests whose job it is to maintain the proper order of the necropolis understand what you have been doing with the objects in their care."

The merchant wrote the address. He wrote it quickly. He did not look at the granite figure as he wrote, which Kha noted. A man who had received many unusual objects in his professional life, who had handled stolen royal burial goods for over a year without apparent spiritual distress, had placed this particular piece in a corner of his back room and had not looked at it since. Whatever he had felt when he first handled it had been enough to keep his eyes away from it.

Kha wrapped the figure in the merchant's own cloth — a length of good Nubian linen that the man offered without being asked — and picked it up. It was heavier than it should have been for its size. Black granite was dense, but this was heavier than density explained. He registered this and filed it and did not think about what the excess weight might consist of.

They walked north along the processional avenue with the wrapped figure under Kha's arm and the address on the ostracon in Nefertari's hand, and neither of them spoke for several minutes because the conversation they needed to have was one that required the privacy of not being surrounded by the entire population of Thebes.

They found a place to sit near the river's edge, away from the landing traffic, where the papyrus reeds provided enough cover for a conversation conducted at low volume. The Nile was bright in full morning now, silver giving way to gold, moving with the steady southern current that would carry it all the way to the delta and eventually to the sea, taking with it the accumulated silt of the flood season, the bodies of fish and birds, the things people dropped in or threw in or entrusted to it in the way that people throughout Egypt's history had always entrusted their most important things to the river, which was the source of everything and therefore the appropriate recipient of everything.

"A shadow sealed in granite," Kha said. "Fed on the power of a royal burial chamber. Now in the hands of a temple singer of Amun." He set the wrapped figure in the reed grass between them and looked at the cloth as though looking at the stone through it. "The canopic jars were taken for their connection to specific dead bodies. The heart scarabs were taken for their proximity to specific dead hearts. Someone is assembling components. The physical remains of the ritual identity of specific dead people, taken from specific tombs in a specific order, collected over years, and now — a shadow. Sealed and fed and ready for collection." He looked at Nefertari. "What are you assembling, when you have someone's shadow and their organs and their heart scarabs?"

Nefertari was quiet for a moment. The river moved past them. A small skiff went by with a man in it who was not looking at anything except the opposite bank, doing the ordinary work of the crossing, in the way of people who made the crossing every day and had stopped registering what the river was.

"In the theology," she said slowly, "the reunification of the parts of the soul is the condition of resurrection. The Ka returns to the body. The Ba is freed to move between the tomb and the world. The Akh is formed by their union. The shadow persists as a kind of anchor, a weight that keeps the resurrected soul attached to the world rather than fully passing on." She was speaking as a scholar now, with the precision of someone working through a text rather than a crisis. "The full resurrection — the kind performed for Osiris by Isis, the kind that is the model for all royal funerary rites — requires all five parts to be present and correctly bound to each other." She looked at the wrapped figure. "If you had someone's canopic jars, their heart scarabs, their shadow — if you had all five components and you had a priest with the knowledge to perform the binding —"

"You could resurrect them," Kha said.

"You could try," she said. "The question of whether it would actually work is — that is a theological question above my training. But the attempt. The ritual attempt. That is what these components are for." She paused. "Which means someone wants someone specifically raised. Not generically. Not experimentally. They know whose components they are collecting, they have been collecting them deliberately and in sequence, and they are close to complete."

"The three remaining tombs," Kha said. "The ones with the false inspection codes but untouched goods. One of those three tombs contains the last components."

"Yes."

"And whatever is in the figure goes to the temple singer today, and from there —" He stopped. "Why a temple singer? Why not deliver it directly to whoever is running this?"

"Because whoever is running this cannot be seen to receive it," Nefertari said. "The temple singer is the last insulating layer. Between the priest who placed the figure in the tomb and the person who ultimately takes possession of all the collected components, there is an entire system of intermediaries — the merchant, the temple singer, perhaps others we have not found. Each one knows only their own piece. None of them know the whole." She looked at him. "Except Usermontu. He is the only person in this chain who has the ritual knowledge to know what the components are actually for. Everyone else thinks they are handling antiques, or political contraband, or something vaguely forbidden but mundanely so. Only the priest understands what he has been building."

"Then we need Usermontu," Kha said. "Today. Before the collection party goes to the merchant and finds the figure gone and understands that someone is moving against them."

"The mortuary temple of Ramesses III at Medinet Habu," Nefertari said. "West bank, south of the valley. Two hours' walk from the south ferry landing." She was already standing, already calculating. "We go back across now. We use my movement authorization to enter the temple precinct. We find Usermontu before he is warned."

"And if he has already been warned?"

She picked up the cloth-wrapped figure from the reed grass, looked at it for a moment, and put it in her document satchel. The satchel settled against her hip, heavier than it should have been, and she adjusted the strap. "Then we have something he will want very much to get back," she said. "Which gives us a conversation regardless."

The second crossing was different from the first. The river was fully lit now, the gold of late morning on the water, the western cliffs ahead of them sharp and white against the blue depth of the sky. They sat in the middle of the ferry, away from the other passengers — three farmers returning from market, a temple servant with an empty offering basket, a young boy who was asleep against a rope coil before they had cleared the eastern landing. The boatman stood at the stern and did not look at them.

Kha held the satchel in his lap. He had taken it from Nefertari on the landing without explaining why, and she had let him take it without asking, which told him she had felt it too — the wrongness of the weight, the particular quality of the heaviness, the way the wrapped figure seemed to exert a small constant pressure on whatever held it, not quite a pull, more the weight of something that was paying attention.

He looked at the water. The Nile was deep here at the mid-crossing — deeper than most people walking its banks understood, a darkness under the bright surface that went down into the black mud of the flood plain, through the sediment of thousands of years of inundation, through the layers of everything the river had accumulated and deposited over the entirety of human civilization on both its banks. He had heard as a child that the Nile had a floor that no one had ever reached, that it went down past the living world into the Duat itself, that this was why it was sacred — not because the gods had blessed it, but because it literally connected the world of the living to the world of the dead through the simple geometry of its depth.

He had believed this as a child and then not believed it as an adult, in the way of adults who trade mystery for utility.

He was less certain, this morning, that the trade had been a good one.

Beside him, Nefertari was reading from a small papyrus sheet she had produced from her sleeve — personal notes, written in a script so compact it was nearly invisible at normal reading distance. She read with the absolute concentration of someone who had trained herself to work in any conditions, and her lips moved slightly, which was the only indication that she was doing anything other than looking at the river. After a few minutes she folded the sheet back into her sleeve and said, without preamble: "Usermontu was part of the investigation into the tomb robbery scandal under Ramesses IV. Forty years ago. He was a junior priest then — barely initiated. He was called as a witness, not a suspect. His testimony covered the ritual condition of three tombs that had been reported as disturbed. He stated that the heka protections on the tombs had been violated and that the disturbance had caused — he used the phrase an imbalance in the accumulation of the dead. The inquiry scribe noted it without following up."

"An imbalance in the accumulation of the dead," Kha said.

"Yes. He was twenty years old and saying something that no one in the inquiry understood, and so it was written down and ignored." She looked at the western bank approaching. "He has spent forty years understanding it more. Whatever he is doing with these components — whatever this ritual is — it is not improvised. It is the culmination of something he has been building in his mind for a very long time."

"He is not working for the Mayor," Kha said slowly. This had been assembling itself at the back of his mind since the merchant's back room and had now arrived at the front. "Or rather — the Mayor is working for him. The Mayor provided the administrative cover. Nebwenenef provided the bureaucratic mechanism. The merchant provided the market. But the purpose — the selection of the specific objects, the sequence of the taking, the feeding of the figure in the burial chamber — that is not a politician's plan or a merchant's plan. That is a priest's plan. Everything else is scaffolding."

"He is the origin," Nefertari said. "Not a mechanism."

"Yes." He looked at the western cliffs above the approaching bank — the pale limestone that hid the valley, that hid the tombs, that hid the accumulated funerary weight of three hundred years of royal burials. "He said, forty years ago, that there was an imbalance. He has spent forty years addressing it. The robbery scandal under Ramesses IV showed him something — what, I don't know. What can be done when the accumulation of the dead is disturbed. What is possible when the boundary moves." He looked at the satchel in his lap. "He has been patient. He has been operating through layers of people who do not understand what they are serving. And he is now very nearly finished."

The ferry touched the western bank. The boatman secured the rope. The farmers and the temple servant and the sleeping boy all moved toward the landing with the automatic efficiency of people completing a routine, and Kha and Nefertari were the last to step off, as they had been the last to board, in the manner of people who were not quite ready for what the bank they were returning to was going to require of them.

The western cliffs rose above them, white and absolute, containing everything they had come from and everything they were walking back into. The dead lay behind those cliffs in their painted rooms, in their incomplete states, their canopic jars in a merchant's back room on the far bank, their heart scarabs scattered into wealthy households, their shadows sealed in granite and fed on borrowed power. Forty-seven objects confirmed. The actual number higher. And somewhere in the accumulation of the disturbed and the incomplete and the improperly rested, something that a priest of forty years' careful preparation was trying to wake up.

Kha adjusted the satchel on his shoulder and walked south toward Medinet Habu.

Behind them, on the river, a small wave moved against the bank and withdrew, the way the river always moved — constant, indifferent, ancient — and in the disturbance of the water where the ferry had rested, something that was not a reflection turned once, slowly, below the surface, and was gone.

The boatman, who had made this crossing for thirty years and had long since stopped seeing any of it, did not notice. He was already turning the ferry back toward the east.
