Epilogue

The Record That Remains

Deir el-Medina — Three Months After the Binding

"As long as you are remembered, you shall not perish. Your name shall exist on earth for all time."

— Funerary inscription, Eighteenth Dynasty

The official report, when it was filed with the office of the Vizier of Thebes at the end of the audit period, was eleven pages long. It documented six confirmed instances of necropolis record falsification, forty-nine objects recovered and restored to their original tomb designations — the final count two higher than the preliminary estimate, the two additional pieces having been located in the household inventory of a minor treasury official in the temple of Amun who surrendered them without requiring further investigation once the formal inquiry letters arrived. The report named the administrative mechanism by which the falsifications had been accomplished. It named the Mayor of Western Thebes as a person requiring further examination by the appropriate judicial authority. It named a temple accountant called Nebwenenef as the primary architect of the record falsification scheme, noting that this individual had surrendered himself to the inquiry without resistance and had cooperated fully with the reconstruction of the administrative record, providing a written account of his methodology that the inquiry scribes described in their own supplementary notes as unusually complete. It noted, with the impersonal brevity of official language applied to a large and irreducible fact, that the junior auditing scribe Amenhotep-sa-Hapu had died during the inquiry under circumstances warranting further review.

It did not name the priest Usermontu, by agreement with the Vizier's senior religious advisor, who had read a separate and restricted document submitted alongside the official report and had determined that the ritual dimensions of the matter were outside the scope of the civil inquiry and would be addressed through the temple hierarchy's own processes, which were slower and quieter and left considerably less in the public record. It did not name the foreman of the left gang at Deir el-Medina except in the administrative appendix, where his signature appeared on three of the original construction documents that had been used to establish the existence of the hidden passage. It did not name the foreman of the right gang at all.

The official report was accurate in everything it contained. It was incomplete in the way that all official reports are incomplete — shaped by what the institution could receive, edited by what the institution could use, silent about what the institution had no framework to hold. It would be filed in the archive of the Vizier's office and consulted by later administrators trying to understand a period of necropolis mismanagement, and it would give them the facts and none of the texture, the mechanism and none of the weight, the names of the guilty and none of the names of the dead.

Nefertari-Tawy wrote a different document.

— ✦ —

She wrote it in the house she had taken near the south end of the village, the small house with the good north light that she had negotiated on the strength of an extended audit assignment that was technically still active and would remain technically active for as long as the restoration of the forty-nine objects to their tombs required official oversight, which she had assessed would take approximately eight months and had communicated to the Karnak administration in a letter carefully worded to make eight months sound like the minimum rather than the maximum. The house had a low ceiling and a good table and a window that looked out onto the single street of Deir el-Medina, where she could hear the morning sounds of a village going about the work of maintaining itself and the world, as it had been doing for as long as anyone could remember and intended, as far as she could determine, to continue doing indefinitely.

She wrote in the evenings, after the day's official work was done — the object inventories, the tomb condition assessments, the correspondence with the temple administration about the return protocols for the more complex cases where the original burial records had been damaged and the restoration required consultation with senior priests who knew the specific funerary traditions of the relevant periods. She wrote by lamplight with a reed pen and good carbon ink, the kind the House of Life used for documents of formal standing, because what she was writing was a document of formal standing even if no institution would ever formally receive it.

She wrote everything. She wrote it in the order it had happened, from the first evidence Hapu had found in the falsified inspection records through the conversations in the dead scribe's house and the crossing of the river and the merchant's back room and the mortuary temple and the night entry into KV47 and the face in the dark and the retrieval of the components and the binding and the morning after. She wrote it with the precision she had been trained to and the detail that precision alone could not supply, the detail that required having been present and having paid attention and having understood, in real time, that what was happening was not simply an administrative irregularity but something that deserved to be written down completely, for whoever came after, in the way that all true things deserved to be written down for whoever came after.

She wrote the names.

This account was begun by the auditing scribe Amenhotep-sa-Hapu, son of Nebseny the water carrier and Henut the weaver, trained in the House of Life at the temple of Mut at Karnak, who passed the examination of qualification at the age of fourteen years on the first attempt, a distinction achieved by fewer than one in thirty candidates. He conducted this inquiry with rigor and without fear until the inquiry required more of him than a person could give and still remain living. His methodology was correct. His findings were correct. The conclusions he did not live to write are written here in his name, by the hand of his assistant, who was given the extraordinary privilege of being allowed to finish what he started.

This account was continued and completed by Nefertari-Tawy, daughter of Amenhotep the scribe and Isis the temple singer, trained in the House of Life at the temple of Mut at Karnak, auditing scribe of the second rank, currently assigned to the necropolis restoration oversight for the western bank of Thebes.

The following persons contributed to the resolution of the matters described herein, and their contributions are recorded here because the official account does not contain them and the official account is not the complete account:

Kha, son of Amenemhat, son of Hori — foreman of the left gang at Deir el-Medina, third generation of his family in the service of the necropolis, who found the first evidence because he knew the floors he had built and refused, when it mattered, to look away from what the floors were telling him.

Paneb, son of Neferhotep — foreman of the right gang at Deir el-Medina, who warned the foreman of the left gang when warning him was the more dangerous choice, and who stood watch at the south entry of the valley on the night of the binding in the cold and the dark and the complete absence of any reward for doing so.

Panenhesy, son of Ipuia — overseer of the tomb works at Deir el-Medina for twenty-two years, who gave the name of the merchant and the name of the administrator and the ostracon that broke the first wall of the conspiracy, at a moment when giving those things was the first honest act he had performed in seven years and he knew it and gave them anyway.

Usermontu, priest of Sokar, of the mortuary temple of Ramesses III — who built the wrong machine for the right purpose, and who will answer for the building of it to the temple hierarchy, and who walked into KV20 with two strangers and an old cedar carrying case and spoke a permission formula over a tomb that did not know him and whose dead received their components from his hands at the fourth hour of the night with the specific gravity of something that has been owed for a very long time finally being paid.

Nebwenenef, son of Mahu — temple accountant, third rank, of the treasury office of the temple of Amun at Karnak — who was wrong about what he was doing and right about why he was doing it, which is the most difficult kind of person to account for in an official document and the most important kind to account for in a true one. His full written testimony is appended. It is worth reading. Even the parts that are wrong.

— ✦ —

She stopped writing at the point where the list ended and looked at what she had written. The lamp had burned low. Outside the window the street was quiet — the full quiet of the deep night, which in this village had a quality different from the quiet of other places, the quality of a community that lived in the shadow of the dead and had made its accommodation with that fact over generations, the quiet of people who pressed their palms to the floor every morning and named the gods and went to work, and who slept, when the balance was correct, without difficulty.

She was aware that what she had written would not be filed in any official archive. She had considered this from the beginning and had decided, early in the writing, that the absence of an official destination did not diminish the obligation to write it. A record did not require an institution to be a record. A name did not require a temple wall to be a name. The Egyptian understanding of this — the understanding that she had absorbed through years of working with funerary texts and that had become, over the course of these three months, something more than professional knowledge — was that the record and the reality it described were not separate things. The writing was the keeping. The naming was the existence. The document she was completing, sealed in cedar and placed in the specific location she had already chosen — a small cavity in the south wall of the House of Life's archive at Deir el-Medina, behind a loosened stone that a junior archivist with good spatial memory could locate again without difficulty — would be as real as anything the Vizier's office had received. More real, in the ways that mattered. More complete.

She added one page to the document before she sealed it. It was not part of the narrative account. It was a list — the forty-nine names of the objects returned to their tombs, each one written with its tomb designation and its original owner's name and the date of its restoration. Forty-nine entries. Each one a line in a ledger of a debt paid. Each one a small correction to a long imbalance, the kind of correction that did not announce itself and did not require witnesses and was no less real for the absence of ceremony.

The last entry on the list was not an object. It was a name.

She wrote it in the larger, more formal hieroglyphic script rather than the hieratic she had used for the rest of the document — the script reserved for things intended to last, the script cut into stone and painted onto walls and inscribed on the objects placed in sealed rooms for the use of the dead. She wrote it with the care of someone who understood that the writing of a name was a ritual act and that ritual acts deserved to be performed correctly, at full attention, with the weight of intention behind every stroke of the pen.

She wrote: Userhat, son of Kenna — painter, cutter, colleague, friend — who asked his question aloud in a company that was not safe for questions, and whose name has been spoken since his death by one person, in the dark, with the full weight of the naming, every night that it mattered and some nights that it did not. Let it be spoken here also. Let it be written. Let him be known to have existed, and to have had the kind of courage that does not survive its exercise, and to be therefore owed, by the record that remains, the permanence he was denied by the record that preceded it.

She set down the pen. She looked at the name in the lamplight — the hieroglyphs precise and carefully formed, the ink still wet, the name as present on the page as it would ever be anywhere. She thought about the foreman of the left gang who had spoken this name into the desert dark every night for seven years, alone, in the manner of the funerary rites, giving it what weight he could from the only source he had available, which was himself and his refusal to stop. She thought about what that refusal had cost him and what it had preserved and what it had made possible.

She thought: this is what it means to do the work. Not the tomb-cutting or the record-keeping or the binding of the thinning boundary between worlds. The other work. The work of the living on behalf of the dead, and the living on behalf of the living, and the steady accumulation of small honest acts that held the world in the shape the world was supposed to be in. Not forever. Not without damage. Not without the grain ships being late and the records being falsified and the balance tipping and requiring correction. But held. Maintained. Renewed each morning by the people who pressed their palms to the floor and named what they were responsible for naming.

Outside, the street was beginning its first sounds — a door, a child's voice, the rhythm of grain being ground, the smell of ovens taking heat. The village waking. The morning arriving at its proper time, because someone had done the work of the previous night that made the morning possible, as someone had done the night before, and the night before that, as far back as the first craftsman who had pressed his palm to the first cut stone floor and understood in his bones what the pressing of a palm meant.

Nefertari-Tawy, daughter of Amenhotep the scribe and Isis the temple singer, auditing scribe of the second rank, currently assigned to the necropolis restoration oversight for the western bank of Thebes, sealed the document in cedar and set it on the table beside her lamp and looked at it for a moment in the early light.

Then she washed her hands in the clay basin by the door, because cleanliness was not vanity — it was armor — and she picked up the document and went out into the street to find the foreman of the left gang, who would know where to put it.

He did.
